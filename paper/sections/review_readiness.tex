\section{Validity Boundaries}

The evidence in this manuscript supports a methods claim, not a final
population-level claim about legal model quality. The strongest supported claim
is that the pipeline can run end to end from a real source case, generate all
major artifacts without manual patching, preserve provenance, expose
perturbation behavior, cluster observed legal-reasoning signatures, apply a
row-level rubric judge, project centroid scores, and record escalation state.
The unsupported claim would be that the method is already externally validated
across legal domains or that the reported model ranking estimates general model
quality.

The remaining validity questions are concrete. Frank must be tested on held-out
source cases to determine whether it consistently preserves legally controlling
facts and avoids answer leakage. Karthic must be compared with expert-authored
rubrics to quantify row coverage, source support, and double-counting. Dasha
must be evaluated on larger natural-response batches across multiple doctrines
and compared with alternative clustering strategies such as embedding-based
clustering or human-coded reasoning labels. Judge must be calibrated against
expert legal scores to estimate row-level reliability and bias. Zak thresholds
must be tuned so escalation captures genuine uncertainty without overwhelming
the review process.

The current evidence is therefore sufficient for independent technical review
of the method and implementation. It is not sufficient for a broad empirical
claim that the pipeline is valid across all legal domains. That broader claim
requires held-out source packs, expert annotations, reliability thresholds
specified before evaluation, and statistical analysis over cases rather than a
single calibration source.
