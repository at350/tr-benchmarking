\begin{abstract}
We study the problem of evaluating legal reasoning in large language model
(LLM) answers when correctness depends on the path through a doctrine, not only
on the final conclusion. We introduce a source-grounded evaluation pipeline that
constructs a benchmark instance from a legal source case, elicits natural model
answers, clusters those answers by inferred legal-reasoning signatures, and
scores cluster representatives with a case-specific rubric. The pipeline
contains five artifact-producing modules: Frank generates a source-grounded
question, gold answer, and perturbations; Karthic derives a row-level rubric
from the locked Frank packet; Dasha maps each natural answer to a normalized
legal-reasoning signature and forms centroids; Judge applies the rubric to
centroid representatives; and Zak records uncertainty or disagreement requiring
escalation.

We validate the method on a real Illinois Statute-of-Frauds case,
\emph{Anglemire v. Policemen's Benevolent Association of Chicago}. The live run
uses ten response-model identifiers, two samples per model, and three
Frank-generated question tracks: the original scenario, a legally invariant
party-name edit, and a material signed-writing edit. Across 60 unlabeled
natural answers, Dasha formed 23 track-aware reasoning centroids with complete
member coverage (Wilson 95\% CI for member/centroid coherence:
0.940--1.000). The invariant edit preserved the dominant reasoning family,
whereas the material edit shifted the dominant answer family with a categorical
distribution change under a permutation test (total variation distance 1.000,
\(p=0.0005\)). A two-model judge panel produced 230 final row-level scores;
18 rows required adjudication, and pre-adjudication agreement was moderate
(weighted \(\kappa=0.630\), mean absolute error 0.465). Projected centroid
scores produced model rankings, but bootstrap intervals overlapped at the top
of the ranking. A separate 500-response deterministic regression suite verifies
cluster bookkeeping and score projection over eight Statute-of-Frauds reasoning
archetypes. The results support a bounded claim: the pipeline is reproducible,
artifact-complete, perturbation-aware, and internally valid for the reported
case study. Broader legal validity requires held-out cases and external expert
review.
\end{abstract}
