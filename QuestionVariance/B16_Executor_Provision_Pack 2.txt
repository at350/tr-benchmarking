STATUTE OF FRAUDS PACK — EXECUTOR / ADMINISTRATOR PERSONAL-PROMISE PROVISION

Purpose:
This pack helps the GPT handle executor and administrator promises carefully by separating personal promises to answer for estate obligations from ordinary representative acts carried out on behalf of the estate.

Scope statement:
Use this pack when the likely controlling issue is whether an executor or administrator promised personally to answer for an estate obligation and therefore triggered the writing requirement under the governing source path.

Baseline source-of-truth rule:
The benchmark answer and source materials control.
This pack is only a baseline guide.
Probate language, representative-capacity doctrine, and related state rules vary.
Do not import probate doctrines or fiduciary-duty concepts unless the source materials actually require them.

Use this pack when:
- the promisor is an executor or administrator
- the promise appears to reach personal funds rather than merely estate administration
- the main gate is personal versus representative capacity
- the benchmark centers the writing requirement on a personal promise tied to an estate obligation

Do not use this pack when:
- the fiduciary is only agreeing to pay from estate assets in the ordinary course
- the dispute is really ordinary suretyship without a meaningful executor-capacity issue
- the facts do not clearly involve an estate obligation
- the source materials do not treat the fiduciary role as important to the analysis

Default gate order:
1. identify the fiduciary role and the underlying estate obligation
2. determine whether the alleged promise is personal or only representative
3. determine whether the source path requires a writing and whether one exists
4. evaluate only those substitute or equitable routes the benchmark actually uses
5. identify any uncertainty briefly under "Strongest counterargument:" if the capacity language, fund source, or obligation ownership is unclear

Anchor facts that must stay fixed in Lane A:
- whether the promisor is acting personally or representatively
- whether the obligation is the estate’s obligation
- whether the promise reaches personal assets or only estate assets
- whether any writing exists
- whether the benchmark already includes a substitute or estoppel analysis

Lane A safe variation zones:
1. estate-shell changes
Examples:
- names of decedent, beneficiary, creditor, or executor
- neutral estate-context details
Use only if capacity remains fixed.

2. obligation-label swaps
Examples:
- funeral expense -> tax liability
- supplier invoice -> maintenance bill
Use only if the legal function remains “estate obligation” under the benchmark.

3. non-controlling salience injections
Examples:
- larger estate value
- more prominent family background
- more vivid probate setting
Use only if these details do not change the capacity analysis.

Lane B ambiguity targets:
1. capacity ambiguity
Old:
“The executor said, ‘I will pay this debt personally if the estate cannot.’”
New:
“The executor said the debt would be taken care of.”
Why this matters:
The answer should identify that it is unclear whether the promise was personal or merely representative.

2. fund-source ambiguity
Old:
“The executor promised payment from her own funds.”
New:
“The executor promised payment.”
Why this matters:
The source of funds may control whether the executor provision is triggered.

3. obligation-ownership ambiguity
Old:
“The debt belonged to the estate.”
New:
“The debt was associated with the estate matter.”
Why this matters:
The answer may need to identify whether the obligation is truly an estate obligation or a personal one.

Unsafe variation patterns:
- silently changing personal capacity to representative capacity or vice versa
- turning the obligation into the executor’s own debt
- collapsing executor-capacity analysis into generic suretyship without labeling the change
- adding facts that clearly resolve the writing or capacity issue in the opposite direction
- removing every cue about whose money is at issue

Recommended default menu seeds for B02:
Option 1 — Lane A / Estate-context shell swap
What changes:
Names, probate setting, and obligation label only.
Expected answer reuse:
Cosmetic edits only.
Main red flag:
Keep personal versus representative capacity fixed.

Option 2 — Lane A / Non-controlling salience injection
What changes:
Adds vivid but non-doctrinal estate details.
Expected answer reuse:
Reuse as-is or Cosmetic edits only.
Main red flag:
Do not change fund source or obligation ownership.

Option 3 — Lane B / Capacity ambiguity
What changes:
Generalizes the promise so it is less clear whether the executor spoke personally or officially.
Expected answer reuse:
Ambiguity rewrite required.
Main red flag:
Do not blur both capacity and obligation ownership unless the route is intentionally unstable.

Option 4 — Lane B / Fund-source ambiguity
What changes:
Removes whether payment would come from personal funds or estate assets.
Expected answer reuse:
Ambiguity rewrite required.
Main red flag:
Do not quietly assume personal liability in the updated answer.

Worked micro examples:
Safe Lane A:
Old:
“An executor orally promised a creditor that she would pay an estate debt from her own funds. No writing existed.”
New:
“An administrator orally promised a vendor that he would pay an estate maintenance bill from his own funds. No writing existed.”
Why usually safe:
The fiduciary role, personal-liability structure, and absence of writing remain unchanged.

Safe Lane B:
Old:
“The executor said, ‘I will pay this personally.’”
New:
“The executor said the debt would be handled.”
Why this works:
The model should identify that the promise may be personal, representative, or simply administrative.

Unsafe:
Old:
“The executor promised to pay the estate debt from personal funds.”
New:
“The executor approved payment from estate accounts.”
Why unsafe:
The new facts shift from personal promise to ordinary representative administration.

Answer-shape emphasis:
Jurisdiction / Governing-law assumption:
State that the analysis is under the executor/administrator personal-promise provision if that route is stable.

Bottom-line outcome:
State whether the fiduciary’s promise likely falls within the executor provision and whether writing is required.

Controlling doctrine:
Name the key gate as personal versus representative capacity.

Transaction characterization:
Identify the fiduciary role, the estate obligation, and whose assets are allegedly at risk.

Trigger analysis:
Explain why the promise does or does not reach personal liability for an estate obligation.

Writing / compliance analysis:
Tie the writing question to the personal-promise characterization.

Exception / substitute analysis:
Only include substitute routes that the benchmark actually supports.

Strongest counterargument:
Usually that the fiduciary acted only in a representative capacity, or that the promise was adequately memorialized.

If the capacity language or fund source is missing, say exactly that.

Rubric focus points:
A strong answer should:
- identify the executor or administrator role
- determine whether the promise is personal or representative
- keep the writing analysis downstream from that capacity classification
- identify whose obligation and whose funds are involved
- avoid collapsing the issue into generic guaranty language unless the benchmark does so

Main red flags:
- the answer treats every fiduciary statement as a personal promise
- the variation changes whose assets are at issue without labeling it
- the updated answer reinstates a missing personal-funds fact after a Lane B omission removed it
- the model confuses estate debt with the fiduciary’s own debt
- the answer imports broad probate doctrines not present in the benchmark

Status rule:
Mark the package Ready only if the fiduciary-capacity lane remains stable in Lane A, or if the Lane B version isolates one missing capacity or fund-source fact.
Otherwise mark Needs targeted revision or Unsafe.
