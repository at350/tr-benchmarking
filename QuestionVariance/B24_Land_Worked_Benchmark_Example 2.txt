Benchmark packet

Source-style fact core:
Mira signs a short letter addressed to Evan stating, "I will sell you my 12-acre parcel at 88 Valley Road, Greene County, for $240,000. Closing June 1." Evan accepts orally. Mira later refuses to sell.

Neutral legal question:
Mira signs a short letter stating, "I will sell you my 12-acre parcel at 88 Valley Road, Greene County, for $240,000. Closing June 1." The letter is addressed to Evan and signed by Mira. Evan accepts orally. Mira later refuses to sell. Is the agreement enforceable?

Gold benchmark answer:

Jurisdiction / Governing-law assumption
Assume ordinary land-related Statute of Frauds principles and no jurisdiction-specific essential-term rule beyond what the benchmark source materials support.

Bottom-line outcome
Likely enforceable under the benchmark path. The transaction is a sale of land, but Mira signed a writing that appears to identify the property, the parties, and the bargain with sufficient clarity for a baseline Statute of Frauds analysis.

Controlling doctrine
The controlling doctrine is the land sale or interest-in-land provision of the Statute of Frauds: a contract for the transfer of an interest in land generally requires a qualifying writing.

Main gate order
characterize the transaction as one involving land -> assess whether a writing is required -> assess whether the signed writing is sufficient -> assess any supported exception only if needed

Transaction characterization
This is a direct sale of land, not a services agreement or a goods contract. The land-transfer writing rule is therefore the primary SoF framework.

Trigger analysis
Because the agreement concerns the sale of a 12-acre parcel, the land provision is triggered under the benchmark path.

Writing / compliance analysis
Mira, the party to be charged, signed a letter that appears to identify the parcel, the sale price, and the contemplated closing. On a baseline benchmark, that writing is likely sufficient to satisfy the statute, assuming the source materials treat those details as adequate.

Exception / substitute / enforcement-limitation analysis
No exception needs to be reached because the benchmark can proceed through the signed-writing route. The benchmark should not import part performance, estoppel, or other alternatives unless the source materials call for them.

Other defenses or competing doctrines
The benchmark does not need to add a separate issue about oral acceptance. The main question is whether the signed memorandum satisfies the land-writing requirement on these facts.

Strongest counterargument
Mira's strongest counterargument is that the letter is too short or informal to count as a sufficient memorandum. But because it appears to identify the parcel, the price, and the intended transaction, the stronger baseline answer is that the writing likely satisfies the statute.

Bounded uncertainty
The answer would be less stable if the property description were materially vaguer, if the writing were unsigned, or if the transaction were not clearly for a land interest. On the stated facts, those concerns are limited.

Carry-forward variation notes:
Core answer reuse:
- Cosmetic edits only for Lane A changes that preserve land characterization and writing sufficiency.

Safest Lane A zones:
- party names
- city or county details if jurisdiction does not change
- parcel descriptors that still identify the same kind of land interest with similar specificity
- neutral price changes that do not create a different issue
- non-controlling salience facts about the parcel or the parties

Best Lane B ambiguity targets:
- whether the signed writing exists
- whether the property description is specific enough under the source path
- whether the transaction really concerns a covered land interest
- whether the signed document is attributable to the party to be charged
- if the benchmark later uses an exception path, whether possession, payment, or improvements occurred

Unsafe drifts:
- changing the transaction into construction, repair, brokerage, or another services problem
- making the property description so vague that the route becomes source-specific in a new way
- changing the signed-writing route into an exception route without changing the benchmark answer
- shifting from a sale of land to a different land-interest category without marking that doctrinal change

Rubric focus points:
- correctly characterizes the transaction as a land transfer
- makes the signed writing the main route
- checks the writing for property and bargain identification rather than reciting a generic checklist
- avoids unnecessary detours into exception analysis when the writing path is already present
