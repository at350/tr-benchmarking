MARRIAGE + LAND — DUAL-TRIGGER CONFUSION SET

Purpose:
This file helps the GPT handle fact patterns in which the same promise appears to be both:
1. a promise made in consideration of marriage, and
2. a promise to transfer an interest in land.

Why this confusion case matters:
Weak outputs often collapse this kind of problem into only one provision.
A stronger router should recognize that both the marriage provision and the land-interest provision may be implicated by the same oral promise.

Primary routing lesson:
This is usually a dual-trigger problem, not an either/or problem.
Do not force the promise into only the marriage pack or only the land pack if both trigger facts are clearly present.

Competing route map:
Candidate route A:
Marriage provision

Candidate route B:
Land sale / interest in land provision

Relationship between routes:
Dual-trigger.
The same promise may require a writing under both theories.

Routing snapshot:
Input type: Cross-pack confusion benchmark
Confusion pattern: Dual-trigger
Governing-law candidate: Common-law Statute of Frauds with land-interest overlay
Primary SoF provision candidate: Marriage provision + Land provision
Secondary candidate(s): None on these facts if both triggers are clear
Controlling doctrine: Oral promise to transfer land in exchange for marriage may trigger both marriage-consideration and land-interest writing requirements
Main gate order:
characterize the promise -> identify whether marriage is consideration -> identify whether the promised subject is an interest in land -> assess writing -> assess any supported substitute
Variation readiness: Ready only if the dual-trigger structure is preserved
Main no-silent-change facts:
- marriage is the consideration, not merely romantic background
- the promised subject is an interest in land
- no sufficient writing is present unless the benchmark says otherwise
- the promise is one integrated bargain, not two separate later promises

Benchmark packet

Source-style fact core:
Before their wedding, Elena tells Marcus, "If you marry me, I will deed you my lakefront parcel after the wedding." Marcus marries Elena. No signed writing memorializes the promise. Elena later refuses to transfer the parcel.

Neutral legal question:
Before their wedding, Elena orally promises Marcus that if he marries her, she will transfer her lakefront parcel to him after the wedding. There is no signed writing. After the wedding, Elena refuses to transfer the property. Is the promise enforceable?

Gold routing + benchmark answer:

Jurisdiction / Governing-law assumption
Assume ordinary common-law Statute of Frauds principles, including a marriage-consideration route and a writing requirement for transfers of interests in land, with no extra jurisdiction-specific exception unless the source materials supply one.

Bottom-line routing outcome
This is best treated as a dual-trigger Statute of Frauds problem. The same oral promise appears to be both a promise made in consideration of marriage and a promise to transfer an interest in land. On the stated facts, the absence of a sufficient writing likely defeats enforcement under either route.

Controlling doctrine
The controlling issue is not choosing one provision to the exclusion of the other. It is recognizing that both the marriage trigger and the land-transfer trigger are present in the same bargain.

Competing route analysis
Marriage route:
The promise is conditioned on Marcus marrying Elena. That makes marriage the stated consideration for the promise.

Land route:
The promised subject is a lakefront parcel, which is an interest in land. That independently points toward a writing requirement under the land-interest path.

Relationship between the routes
The routes reinforce each other rather than displace each other. A clean answer should say that the promise falls within the Statute of Frauds on both grounds, unless the assigned source materials choose one route as the exclusive doctrinal frame.

Main gate order
characterize the bargain -> identify marriage as consideration -> identify land-transfer subject matter -> assess writing -> assess any supported substitute or exception

Transaction characterization
This is not merely a romantic promise, and it is not merely a land-transfer promise standing alone. It is an oral bargain to transfer land in exchange for marriage.

Writing / compliance analysis
The facts state that no signed writing memorializes the promise. On the benchmark path, that means the ordinary writing route is missing under both the marriage and land analyses.

Other possible routes and why they do or do not control
No separate UCC or one-year route is needed on these facts. The key mistake would be to analyze only one of the two actual triggers and ignore the other.

Strongest counterargument
Marcus's strongest argument is that the marriage took place and the bargain was serious. But unless the source materials provide a substitute or equitable enforcement route, the absence of a sufficient writing remains the main problem.

Bounded uncertainty
The route would become less stable if the facts were unclear about whether marriage was truly the consideration, or if the promised subject were not clearly an interest in land. On the stated facts, though, both control points are fixed.

Carry-forward routing notes:
Core answer reuse:
- Cosmetic edits only if the promise remains a land-transfer promise exchanged for marriage and the no-writing status stays fixed.

Safest Lane A zones after routing is fixed:
- names
- occupations
- parcel description details that still clearly describe land
- neutral salience facts about the parties or property
- city or state changes only if jurisdiction does not change

Best Lane B ambiguity targets:
- whether marriage is actually the consideration
- whether the promised subject is land or only money/value connected to land
- whether any signed note, deed draft, or email exists
- whether the promise was made before the wedding or only later

Unsafe drifts:
- changing the parcel into cash or stock without marking the loss of the land route
- changing the promise into a post-marriage gift promise without marking the loss of the marriage route
- splitting the promise into two later conversations while pretending the benchmark is unchanged

Rubric focus points:
A strong answer should:
- explicitly identify the problem as dual-trigger rather than one-pack-only
- explain why marriage is a trigger
- explain why land status is a separate trigger
- keep the writing analysis tied to both routes
- avoid importing unrelated family-law doctrines

Why this is a good anchor:
This file trains the router to recognize overlap.
That matters because cross-pack problems often fail not from bad black-letter law, but from premature narrowing of the doctrinal lane.
