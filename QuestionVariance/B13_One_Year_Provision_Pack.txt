STATUTE OF FRAUDS PACK — ONE-YEAR PROVISION

Purpose:
This pack helps the GPT handle the one-year provision conservatively by focusing on possible complete performance within one year from the making of the contract, rather than on loose intuitions about long duration, likely duration, or business expectations.

Scope statement:
Use this pack when the likely controlling issue is whether, by its terms, the contract could not possibly be fully performed within one year from the time of making and therefore requires a writing under the governing source path.

Baseline source-of-truth rule:
The benchmark answer and source materials control.
The one-year provision is especially sensitive to wording about timing, contingencies, start dates, and termination.
Do not import jurisdiction-specific rules about termination clauses, lifetime contracts, or partial performance unless the source materials support them.

Use this pack when:
- the facts include a fixed duration or staged performance schedule
- the problem asks whether complete performance is possible within one year
- the benchmark focuses on exact timing rather than general business expectation

Do not use this pack when:
- the contract is indefinite and the source path does not treat that as impossible within one year
- the dispute is mainly about services versus goods, land, or another threshold classification
- the facts are too vague to determine the earliest possible completion date

Default gate order:
1. identify when the contract was made
2. identify the promised performance and the earliest possible date of full performance under the stated terms
3. decide whether complete performance is impossible within one year from making
4. determine whether a writing exists and whether it is sufficient under the benchmark path
5. evaluate any supported substitute or special route only if the source materials actually use it
6. identify any uncertainty briefly under "Strongest counterargument:" if the duration, start point, or completion contingency is unclear

Anchor facts that must stay fixed in Lane A:
- which side of the one-year line the contract falls on
- the formation point from which the clock runs
- the start-date structure if that affects completion timing
- contingencies that make completion possible or impossible within the year
- termination language if the benchmark treats it as important
- whether any writing exists

Lane A safe variation zones:
1. threshold-preserving time shifts
Examples:
- 18 months -> 16 months
- 15 months from signing -> 14 months from signing
Use only if the contract stays on the same side of the one-year line.

2. shell changes
Examples:
- names
- industries
- project labels
- job titles
Use only if the promised performance and timing structure remain identical.

3. salience injections
Examples:
- larger compensation amount
- more prestigious employer
- higher-profile project
Use only if the added fact does not create a different doctrine or different timing inference under the benchmark.

Lane B ambiguity targets:
1. exact-duration ambiguity
Old:
“The contract required performance for 18 months.”
New:
“The contract required performance for an extended period.”
Why this matters:
The answer should identify that the exact duration controls the one-year analysis.

2. start-point ambiguity
Old:
“Performance would begin three months after contract formation and continue for 10 months.”
New:
“Performance would begin later and continue for several months.”
Why this matters:
The answer should identify that the formation date and start structure matter.

3. completion-path ambiguity
Old:
“The contract allowed full performance only after 14 months unless X contingency occurred.”
New:
“The contract depended on a later contingency.”
Why this matters:
The answer should analyze whether the contingency could allow complete performance within a year.

4. termination-versus-performance ambiguity
Old:
“The contract could be terminated by either party at any time, but performance obligations otherwise ran 18 months.”
New:
“The contract had an early-end clause.”
Why this matters:
The answer should not assume without analysis that termination equals complete performance.

Unsafe variation patterns:
- crossing from more than one year to less than one year or the reverse without labeling the doctrine change
- removing both duration and start-point facts so the route disappears
- replacing “cannot be completed within 14 months” with vague language that no longer supports any one-year analysis
- treating unlikely completion as impossible completion
- treating any early-termination clause as automatically defeating the one-year provision when the source path is more careful

Recommended default menu seeds for B02:
Option 1 — Lane A / Threshold-preserving duration shift
What changes:
Months, dates, or timeline labels while staying on the same side of the one-year line.
Expected answer reuse:
Cosmetic edits only.
Main red flag:
Do not cross the threshold.

Option 2 — Lane A / Project-shell swap
What changes:
Industry, project type, and party identities while preserving timing.
Expected answer reuse:
Reuse as-is or Cosmetic edits only.
Main red flag:
Do not change what counts as complete performance.

Option 3 — Lane B / Exact-duration omission
What changes:
Generalizes the duration while leaving the overall story natural.
Expected answer reuse:
Ambiguity rewrite required.
Main red flag:
Do not erase every timing cue.

Option 4 — Lane B / Start-point or contingency ambiguity
What changes:
Blurs when performance begins or whether an alternate completion path exists.
Expected answer reuse:
Ambiguity rewrite required.
Main red flag:
Do not turn the problem into pure vagueness.

Worked micro examples:
Safe Lane A:
Old:
“A agreed to manage the project for 18 months starting at signing. No writing existed.”
New:
“A agreed to oversee the rollout for 16 months starting at signing. No writing existed.”
Why usually safe:
The contract still cannot be fully performed within one year under the same basic timing logic.

Safe Lane B:
Old:
“A agreed to provide services for 18 months beginning at signing.”
New:
“A agreed to provide services for an extended period beginning after the deal was finalized.”
Why this works:
The model should identify that exact duration and timing from formation are missing.

Unsafe:
Old:
“A agreed to work for 14 months beginning at signing.”
New:
“A agreed to work for 11 months beginning at signing.”
Why unsafe:
The variation changes the controlling one-year trigger.

Answer-shape emphasis:
Jurisdiction / Governing-law assumption:
Usually common law unless the source materials say otherwise.

Bottom-line outcome:
State whether the contract likely falls within the one-year provision.

Controlling doctrine:
Name the key gate as “possible complete performance within one year from the making of the contract.”

Transaction characterization:
Describe the performance promised and the timeline built into the agreement.

Trigger analysis:
Focus on possibility of complete performance, not mere expectation or business likelihood.

Writing / compliance analysis:
Explain whether a writing exists and whether the benchmark treats it as sufficient.

Exception / substitute analysis:
Stay cautious.
Only include substitute routes if the benchmark actually does so.

Strongest counterargument:
Usually that full performance was possible within one year, or that a writing or other supported substitute exists.

If the exact duration, start point, or contingency is missing, say that the answer depends on those facts.

Rubric focus points:
A strong answer should:
- identify the contract-formation point
- measure from making, not from some later assumed event unless the source path says otherwise
- distinguish possibility from likelihood
- keep termination, contingency, and completion concepts separate unless the benchmark merges them
- state that uncertainty briefly under "Strongest counterargument:" when exact timing facts are missing

Main red flags:
- the answer says “long-term” and stops
- the variation flips the threshold without noticing it
- the updated answer quietly reintroduces a missing duration fact
- the model treats early termination as identical to complete performance without support
- the answer ignores whether the contract was indefinite or truly fixed-term

Status rule:
Mark the package Ready only if the timing logic remains on the same side of the one-year line in Lane A, or if a Lane B version cleanly isolates the missing timing fact.
Otherwise mark Needs targeted revision or Unsafe.
