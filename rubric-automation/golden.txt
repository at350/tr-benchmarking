GOLDEN ANSWER 
(benchmark answer and reasoning based on U.S. common law and Restatement (Contracts))

LIKELY RESULT: (condensed final answer) The father’s oral promise is not enforceable (because it falls within a Statute of Frauds category requiring a writing, namely, a contract “in consideration of marriage,” and no writing exists).

REASONING:
I. Contract formation: was there a bargain?
Offer/acceptance: The father made a conditional promise: if the son married the politician’s daughter within 18 months, the father would assume responsibility for the son’s student loans. That is an offer capable of acceptance by performance (a unilateral contract structure). The son’s marriage within the specified period is acceptance by performance.
Consideration v.s. conditional gift: The harder formation question is whether the marriage is bargained-for consideration (the “price” of the father’s promise) or merely a condition on a gratuitous promise (a conditional gift).
Facts supporting bargain/consideration: the father expressly conditioned his promise on the son marrying a particular person within a set time; the marriage is the requested performance.
Fact cutting against bargain (and against reliance later): the son “agreed because he already planned to propose,” which suggests the promise may not have induced the marriage. However, prior intent does not automatically defeat consideration if the act is still demanded as the exchange; it just creates an argument that this is more like a family-gift condition than a true bargain.
Assuming arguendo there is consideration (or at least a colorable contract), enforceability still turns on the Statute of Frauds.
II. Statute of Frauds “gates” (enforceability, not proof)
Gate A: Contract in consideration of marriage (dispositive here*)
Under the traditional Statute of Frauds, a promise is unenforceable unless in a signed writing when it is a contract made upon consideration of marriage, other than mutual promises to marry.
This promise fits that category: the father promised to pay the son’s loans in exchange for the son marrying a specific person within 18 months. It is not a mutual promise between the spouses to marry; it is a third-party promise where marriage is the consideration.
Because the father and son never signed any written contract, the father’s promise is within the marriage-consideration Statute of Frauds and therefore unenforceable.
The son’s full performance (the marriage) does not automatically remove this category from the Statute of Frauds; the writing requirement still applies unless the jurisdiction recognizes a specific substitute doctrine (discussed below).
Gate B: One-year provision (not a bar here)
Although the condition window is 18 months, the one-year Statute of Frauds applies only if the agreement cannot possibly be performed within one year at the time it is made. Here, the son could have married within one year, so the one-year provision does not apply—even though he actually married in 14 months.
Gate C: Suretyship (promise to pay another’s debt; secondary and nuanced)
There is also a potential suretyship issue because the father promised to pay an existing debt (student loans). But classic suretyship Statute of Frauds analysis typically turns on whether the promise is made to the creditor to answer for another’s debt.
On these facts, the promise is made to the son (the debtor), not to the lender. That weakens (and in many treatments defeats) the classic “suretyship” Statute of Frauds trigger.
If the father had separately promised the lender to pay or guarantee the loans, that would be within suretyship and ordinarily require a writing (subject to exceptions like “main purpose”).
If the father intended to “assume” the loans in the sense of a novation/assumption making him primarily liable to the lender, that generally would require creditor assent and often a writing as well.
Even if suretyship did apply, it would not fix the separate, independent marriage-consideration Statute of Frauds problem.
III. Exceptions/substitutes
“Main purpose”/“leading object” doctrine (only relevant if suretyship applies)
If a suretyship writing requirement were triggered (e.g., promise to creditor), the “main purpose” exception may remove the promise from that suretyship category if the father’s dominant purpose was to secure a personal economic benefit. The father’s hoped-for tax deduction is a pecuniary motive, but courts vary on whether that is “direct” enough.
Critically: even a successful main-purpose argument would only address suretyship, not the separate marriage-consideration writing requirement. So it is not dispositive here.
Promissory estoppel (possible but weak on these facts; often limited against SoF)
The son might argue promissory estoppel to enforce an oral promise to avoid injustice. But two major problems:
Inducement/reliance is weak: the prompt says the son already planned to propose. That undermines the claim that the promise caused the marriage.
SoF override is jurisdiction-sensitive: many courts are cautious about using promissory estoppel to circumvent Statute of Frauds categories—especially where the category is designed to require formal proof (like marriage-related bargains). Even where estoppel is allowed, remedies may be limited to what justice requires rather than full expectation.
Given those limits and the weak reliance showing, estoppel is an unlikely path to enforce this promise.
IV. Father’s tax-deduction mistake
The father’s later discovery that he did not qualify for a deduction is generally a unilateral mistake about an expected benefit. Unless the promise was expressly conditioned on actually receiving the deduction (it was not stated as such), the mistake does not excuse performance. But this point matters only if the promise were otherwise enforceable.
V. Conclusion
The father’s oral promise is likely unenforceable because it is a promise made in consideration of marriage, which falls within the Statute of Frauds and requires a signed writing, and none exists. The one-year provision does not apply, and suretyship/main-purpose issues (even if relevant) do not overcome the independent marriage-consideration writing requirement. Promissory estoppel is an uncertain and factually weak fallback given the son’s prior intent to marry.
