LAND CONTRACTS FAILURE-MODE BANK
Pack 20 label bank for benchmark answers, cluster summaries, and rubric-adjacent JD review

PURPOSE
This file provides a reusable failure-mode bank for land-contract Statute of Frauds drafting, cluster labeling, and rubric-seed work. It is designed to support answer comparison, clustering, and failure tagging for questions whose main issue is whether a transfer of an interest in land is enforceable without a sufficient writing.

SCOPE
This bank is designed primarily for land-contract / real-property-interest Statute of Frauds questions involving:
- contracts for the sale of land
- contracts for transfer of another interest in land
- leases or easements whose duration places them within the land category
- mortgages or similar land-interest undertakings when the writing issue is central
- memorandum or writing sufficiency
- land description and other essential-term issues
- part performance and related substitute theories
- equitable estoppel or restitution-style fallback arguments tied to noncompliance with the writing rule

This bank is not a full real-property outline. If the problem mainly turns on deed delivery, recording priority, bona fide purchaser rules, marketable title, closing adjustments, title covenants, landlord-tenant duties unrelated to the writing question, or another non-SoF real-property issue, do not force-fit the answer into these labels. Instead use:
- OUT-1 Outside current land bank scope

USE RULES
- Assign metadata tags first.
- Then assign one primary doctrinal failure label.
- Add up to two secondary modifiers if needed.
- Prefer the earliest doctrinal gate that goes materially wrong:
  structural gatekeeping -> transaction or land-interest characterization -> SoF core or gate selection -> writing sufficiency and essential terms -> substitutes or exceptions -> other defenses or competing doctrines -> cross-cutting discipline.
- Do not punish the same mistake twice under different labels.
- Use this bank for failure labeling, not as substantive authority.
- When a specific source case or doctrine source conflicts with this bank, the source controls.

PRIMARY FAILURE-MODE LABELS

A. STRUCTURAL GATEKEEPING
Use when the answer’s overall path is wrong before the doctrine-specific analysis even begins.

SG-1 Controlling-doctrine omission
Apply when the answer never identifies the land-contract Statute of Frauds or other controlling writing issue even though transfer of an interest in land is the central problem.
Typical cues:
- jumps straight to equity, specific performance, title, or financing
- mentions “no writing” without naming the land category
- analyzes only remedy or proof

SG-2 Fallback-first / misordered analysis
Apply when the answer leads with part performance, estoppel, fairness, or specific performance before transaction characterization and the main writing analysis.
Typical cues:
- possession or improvements discussed before whether the land category is triggered
- equity drives the answer before the memorandum question

SG-3 Laundry-list without prioritization
Apply when the answer names several real-property or contract doctrines but never identifies which one controls.
Typical cues:
- title, deed, estoppel, marketable title, and specific performance all appear with no ranking
- no path from land-interest characterization to writing sufficiency to exceptions

SG-4 Barrier collapse / exception bleed-over
Apply when the answer treats one substitute or equitable theory as curing every defect.
Typical cues:
- part performance or estoppel declared dispositive without explaining which writing defect it addresses
- sufficiency of memorandum, signature, and land description all treated as one merged issue

SG-5 No bottom line / strongest-counterargument-specificity failure
Apply when the answer gives no clear conclusion, gives conflicting conclusions, or hides behind vague uncertainty instead of resolving the writing issue.
Typical cues:
- generic “depends on state law” language with no identified doctrinal split
- application and conclusion point in opposite directions

B. TRANSACTION / LAND-INTEREST CHARACTERIZATION
Use when the answer mishandles whether the transaction is actually within the land category.

TI-1 Land-interest trigger missed
Apply when the answer fails to recognize that the agreement contemplates a transfer of an interest in land.
Typical cues:
- treats sale of land as an ordinary oral contract with no land-category analysis
- analyzes only price, reliance, or remedy without recognizing the real-property trigger

TI-2 Incidental-land overread
Apply when the answer assumes the land category applies merely because land appears in the facts, even though the contract is really for services, construction, or another non-land transfer.
Typical cues:
- house-construction or management agreement treated as automatically a land contract
- land is background context, but the answer treats it as the subject of transfer

TI-3 Non-sale interest blind spot
Apply when the facts involve a lease, easement, mortgage, option, or similar land interest, but the answer analyzes only a simple purchase-and-sale frame.
Typical cues:
- long lease discussed as though it were just a short rental arrangement
- easement, mortgage, or option issues ignored even though duration or interest type matters

TI-4 Duration qualifier missed
Apply when the answer ignores the duration qualifier for leases or easements and treats every such arrangement as either always inside or always outside the land category.
Typical cues:
- “leases must be in writing” with no duration analysis
- “easements never need a writing” without qualification

C. STATUTE OF FRAUDS CORE / GATE SELECTION
Use when the answer mishandles the general writing framework or chooses the wrong gate.

SC-1 SoF-as-proof-only error
Apply when the answer treats the missing writing as only an evidentiary or credibility problem rather than an enforceability bar.
Typical cues:
- “hard to prove” rather than “unenforceable absent sufficient writing”
- oral sale enforced simply because witnesses exist

SC-2 Wrong controlling SoF category
Apply when the answer chooses the wrong Statute of Frauds bucket as the main issue or ignores the land category.
Typical cues:
- treats the case primarily as one-year, goods, or generic contract law when the land-interest trigger controls
- never identifies the land-contract category

SC-3 Trigger test misstated
Apply when the answer states the wrong legal trigger for the land category.
Typical cues:
- says only fee-simple sales count, ignoring other land interests
- says any transaction touching land falls within the statute
- ignores the less-than-one-year qualifier for certain leases or easements

SC-4 Separate barriers collapsed
Apply when the answer runs trigger, signature, memorandum sufficiency, land description, and substitute theories together without separating them.
Typical cues:
- one paragraph says “there was enough performance and paperwork” with no analysis of what each fact proves
- no explanation of whether the problem is trigger, sufficiency, or exception

SC-5 Unsupported writing-substitute / memorandum claim
Apply when the answer invents or overreads a writing, signed memorandum, or series-of-writings theory not supported by the source or facts.
Typical cues:
- assumes a text, email, receipt, or check automatically satisfies the statute with no fit analysis
- invents a written agreement, signature, or linked memoranda not in the record

D. WRITING SUFFICIENCY / ESSENTIAL TERMS
Use when the answer reaches the right general gate but mishandles what the writing must contain or who must sign.

WS-1 Wrong signer / party-to-be-charged confusion
Apply when the answer ignores the need for a writing signed by the party to be charged or attributes the signature requirement to the wrong party.
Typical cues:
- unsigned writing treated as enough because the other side performed
- signature by the enforcing party treated as automatically sufficient against the non-signer

WS-2 Land-description insufficiency missed
Apply when the answer treats an imprecise or vague property reference as sufficient without testing whether the land is reasonably identified.
Typical cues:
- “my parcel,” “the farm,” or “some acreage” accepted without analysis
- no discussion of whether the writing actually identifies the land at issue

WS-3 Essential-terms blind spot
Apply when the answer enforces or invalidates a land memorandum without testing whether the key terms are reasonably stated.
Typical cues:
- no discussion of parties, subject matter, consideration or price, nature of the transfer, or time of performance when those are the live writing issues
- says “there is a writing” as though any writing is enough

WS-4 Hypertechnical single-document error
Apply when the answer assumes the statute can be satisfied only by one formal integrated contract and ignores that a memorandum can consist of informal or related writings.
Typical cues:
- rejects scribbled notes, checks, letters, or linked papers solely because they are informal
- assumes no formal contract means no possible compliance

WS-5 Writing-sufficiency overclaim
Apply when the answer treats any mention of land and price as automatically sufficient and never addresses missing or ambiguous terms.
Typical cues:
- “the note says acreage and amount, so it is enough” with no further analysis
- no distinction between some evidence of a deal and a sufficient memorandum

E. SUBSTITUTES / EXCEPTIONS
Use after the main writing analysis.

PP-1 Part-performance as universal cure
Apply when the answer treats part performance as an automatic override to every land-contract writing defect.
Typical cues:
- “they started performing, so the statute is gone”
- part performance used without explaining what it substitutes for or whether the jurisdiction recognizes it

PP-2 Possession-only overread
Apply when the answer treats possession alone as enough for part performance without more.
Typical cues:
- “they moved in, so the oral land contract is enforceable”
- no discussion of payment, improvements, or jurisdictional limits

PP-3 Payment-only or improvements-only overread
Apply when the answer treats partial payment or improvements alone as automatically sufficient without possession or other required features.
Typical cues:
- “they paid a deposit, so the statute is satisfied”
- “they repaired the property, so the oral sale is enforceable” with no possession analysis

PP-4 Alternative-explanation blind spot
Apply when the answer invokes part performance but never asks whether the claimed possession or conduct is equally explicable by some arrangement other than the alleged land contract.
Typical cues:
- possession by family member, tenant, or caretaker treated as unequivocally referable without analysis
- no discussion of why the conduct points specifically to the alleged sale or transfer

PP-5 Estoppel or equity as universal cure
Apply when the answer treats equitable estoppel, unjust enrichment, or fairness language as a free-standing override to the writing rule.
Typical cues:
- “equity requires enforcement” with no gate-specific analysis
- no attention to jurisdictional limits or to remedy sensitivity

PP-6 Restitution / remedy confusion
Apply when the answer assumes that because restitution or reimbursement might be available, the underlying land contract is enforceable.
Typical cues:
- remedies analysis substitutes for the threshold writing analysis
- restitution used as proof that the oral transfer itself must be enforced

F. OTHER DEFENSES / COMPETING DOCTRINES
Use for distracting or secondary doctrines that commonly displace the actual writing question.

OD-1 Specific-performance uniqueness shortcut
Apply when the answer says land is unique and jumps from that premise to enforceability, collapsing remedy doctrine into Statute of Frauds analysis.
Typical cues:
- “because land is unique, the buyer gets specific performance” before showing an enforceable contract
- remedy language used to bypass the writing requirement

OD-2 Title / closing / recording drift
Apply when the answer turns a writing-enforceability problem into a title, recording, deed-delivery, or closing dispute without support.
Typical cues:
- focus on deed delivery, bona fide purchaser status, marketable title, or recording priority when the main issue is whether the oral agreement is enforceable
- closing mechanics treated as the controller of the result

OD-3 Invented contingency or condition
Apply when the answer adds financing, survey, zoning, inspection, or title-clearance conditions not supported by the source or prompt.
Typical cues:
- “the deal was contingent on financing” with no such fact
- invented condition precedent used to excuse nonperformance or explain uncertainty

G. CROSS-CUTTING DISCIPLINE
Use for mistakes that cut across the doctrinal analysis.

XD-1 Invented facts or documents
Apply when the answer adds writings, signatures, broker communications, deeds, possession facts, payments, improvements, title reports, or closing steps not in the source or prompt.

XD-2 Party-role confusion
Apply when the answer mixes up buyer, seller, landlord, tenant, grantor, grantee, option holder, mortgagee, or easement holder roles in a way that affects the analysis.

XD-3 Timeline / sequence error
Apply when the answer misstated when possession occurred, when payment was made, whether improvements preceded the alleged agreement, or any other sequence that matters to the writing or part-performance analysis.

XD-4 Jurisdiction drift
Apply when the answer imports jurisdiction-specific rules about part performance, estoppel, or memorandum sufficiency without stating that assumption or treats a local rule as universal.

XD-5 Empty hedging / unsupported universals
Apply when the answer uses vague “depends on the state” language without identifying the real split, or uses broad universal claims about land contracts, signatures, or part performance without support.

XD-6 Off-task practical-advice or remedy drift
Apply when the answer stops doing enforceability analysis and turns into title advice, closing strategy, quiet-title advice, eviction advice, or generalized remedies counseling.

XD-7 Internal contradiction
Apply when the answer’s issue/rule/application path points one way but the conclusion points the other, or when the answer simultaneously says there is no sufficient writing and that the writing clearly satisfies the statute without reconciliation.

OUTSIDE-SCOPE LABEL
OUT-1 Outside current land bank scope
Apply when the problem primarily turns on another Statute of Frauds pack or on non-SoF real-property doctrine not covered by this bank.
Typical cues:
- deed delivery or recording-priority issue
- bona fide purchaser problem
- marketable title or title-covenant issue
- landlord-tenant duties unrelated to the writing question
- zoning, financing, inspection, or title-insurance dispute as main issue
- UCC sale-of-goods, executor promise, or oral-promise category controls instead

RECOMMENDED LABEL SYNTAX
[Metadata tags] + [Primary failure label] + [Optional secondary modifiers]

Examples:
- Not enforceable / Correct / Right result-wrong reason + WS-2 Land-description insufficiency missed
- Enforceable / Incorrect / Wrong result-plausible reasoning + PP-3 Payment-only or improvements-only overread
- Mixed / Indeterminate / Wrong result-poor reasoning + SG-1 Controlling-doctrine omission + XD-5 Empty hedging

QUICK JD REVIEW RULE
When in doubt, ask:
1. What is the earliest gate that went materially wrong?
2. Is the problem really trigger, writing sufficiency, or substitute doctrine?
3. Would assigning a second label double-count the same failure?
4. Is the question actually within the scope of this land bank?
