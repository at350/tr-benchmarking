Benchmark packet

Source-style fact core:
On March 1, Hart Consulting orally agrees to provide weekly compliance training to Ridge Labs for sixteen months beginning that same day. No signed writing memorializes the agreement. After several months, Ridge stops paying and argues the agreement is unenforceable.

Neutral legal question:
On March 1, Hart Consulting orally agrees to provide weekly compliance training to Ridge Labs for sixteen months beginning that same day. No signed writing exists. Ridge later refuses to continue paying. Is the agreement enforceable?

Gold benchmark answer:

Jurisdiction / Governing-law assumption
Assume ordinary common-law Statute of Frauds principles and no special jurisdiction-specific rule unless the benchmark source materials provide one.

Bottom-line outcome
Likely not enforceable on the stated facts. Because the contract requires sixteen months of performance beginning on the date of making, complete performance cannot occur within one year, and no sufficient writing is shown.

Controlling doctrine
The controlling doctrine is the one-year provision of the Statute of Frauds: an agreement that, by its terms, cannot be fully performed within one year from the making of the contract generally requires a qualifying writing.

Main gate order
identify when the contract was made -> determine the earliest possible complete performance under the stated terms -> assess writing -> assess any supported substitute or limited enforcement route

Transaction characterization
This is a fixed-term service agreement, not a sale of goods or land transaction. The timing question is therefore the main SoF gate.

Trigger analysis
The agreement calls for weekly compliance training for sixteen months beginning on March 1, the date of contracting. On those terms, full performance cannot be completed within one year from making. That places the contract within the one-year provision under the benchmark path.

Writing / compliance analysis
The facts say there is no signed writing. The ordinary writing route is therefore absent.

Exception / substitute / enforcement-limitation analysis
The benchmark should not assume that partial performance, reliance, or some other substitute automatically removes the one-year problem unless the source materials specifically recognize that route. The facts as stated do not supply a supported substitute.

Other defenses or competing doctrines
No competing doctrine needs to be added. The benchmark issue is timing under the Statute of Frauds, not a dispute about formation, capacity, or UCC classification.

Strongest counterargument
Hart's strongest argument is that the agreement was real and that some performance occurred. But on the benchmark facts, the contract still could not be fully performed within one year from making, and no sufficient writing is shown.

Bounded uncertainty
The result would be less stable if the facts changed the start date, added a contingency that could allow completion within one year, or made the duration indefinite rather than fixed. On the stated facts, the timing line is clear.

Carry-forward variation notes:
Core answer reuse:
- Cosmetic edits only for Lane A changes that preserve the same side of the one-year line and preserve no-writing status.

Safest Lane A zones:
- names
- industry and project labels
- month shifts that stay clearly above one year
- salience facts about contract value or company prestige that do not change the timeline
- place names only if jurisdiction does not change

Best Lane B ambiguity targets:
- exact duration
- exact start point
- whether a contingency could permit completion within one year
- whether the contract is for fixed full performance or indefinite duration
- whether any signed writing exists

Unsafe drifts:
- changing sixteen months to eleven months
- changing the facts so performance might finish within a year
- omitting so much timing detail that the route itself becomes unstable
- smuggling in termination language that changes the doctrine path

Rubric focus points:
- measures from the date of making, not from breach or some later event
- asks whether full performance is possible within one year, not whether it is likely
- centers the missing writing
- avoids importing unsupported substitute doctrines
