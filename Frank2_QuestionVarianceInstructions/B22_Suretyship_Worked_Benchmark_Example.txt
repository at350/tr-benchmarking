Benchmark packet

Source-style fact core:
Beacon Supply orally agrees to continue delivering concrete to Apex Builders. Apex remains responsible for the invoices. Apex's president's mother, Nora, tells Beacon's manager, "Keep delivering. If Apex does not pay, I will pay the balance myself." No signed writing memorializes Nora's statement. Apex later defaults, and Beacon sues Nora personally.

Neutral legal question:
Beacon Supply orally agrees to keep delivering concrete to Apex Builders. Apex's president's mother, Nora, tells Beacon's manager, "If Apex does not pay, I will pay the balance myself." No signed writing exists. Beacon later sues Nora personally after Apex defaults. Is Nora's promise enforceable?

Gold benchmark answer:

Jurisdiction / Governing-law assumption
Assume ordinary common-law Statute of Frauds principles and no jurisdiction-specific exception unless the benchmark source materials provide one.

Bottom-line outcome
Likely not enforceable against Nora on the stated facts. Her statement is best understood as a collateral promise to answer for Apex's debt, and no sufficient writing is shown.

Controlling doctrine
The controlling doctrine is the suretyship provision of the Statute of Frauds: a collateral promise to answer for the debt or default of another generally requires a qualifying writing.

Main gate order
identify the parties and underlying obligation -> decide whether the promise is collateral or original -> assess writing -> assess any supported main-purpose or substitute route

Transaction characterization
Beacon is the creditor. Apex is the underlying obligor. Nora is a third-party promisor. Her statement, as phrased, does not replace Apex's liability; it adds backup liability if Apex does not pay.

Trigger analysis
The promise is facially collateral because Nora says she will pay only if Apex does not. Apex remains the principal debtor. That structure places the promise within suretyship under the benchmark path.

Writing / compliance analysis
The facts state that no signed writing memorializes Nora's undertaking. That means the ordinary writing route is absent.

Exception / substitute / enforcement-limitation analysis
The benchmark facts do not show a clear self-benefit or leading-object route. Nora is not described as receiving a direct commercial benefit from the transaction. The benchmark should not assume a substitute or exception unless the source materials expressly support one.

Other defenses or competing doctrines
No competing doctrine is needed here. The problem is not framed as a novation or direct co-obligor undertaking.

Strongest counterargument
Beacon's strongest counterargument is that Nora's assurance induced continued delivery and might be characterized as a serious business commitment. But the wording still leaves Apex as the principal obligor, so the stronger reading remains collateral.

Bounded uncertainty
The answer would become less stable if the facts showed that Nora made the promise for her own direct business advantage, or if the facts suggested Beacon looked only to Nora for payment. On the given facts, those features are not present.

Carry-forward variation notes:
Core answer reuse:
- Cosmetic edits only for Lane A changes that preserve the collateral structure, addressee, and no-writing status.

Safest Lane A zones:
- names
- business context
- type of underlying debt if legal function stays the same
- neutral dollar shifts that do not create a new threshold or bias problem
- non-controlling salience facts that do not create a direct self-benefit route

Best Lane B ambiguity targets:
- whether the promise was made to the creditor or only to the debtor
- whether Apex remained liable
- whether Nora received a direct business benefit
- whether Nora's statement made her primarily liable
- whether any signed note, email, or guaranty form exists

Unsafe drifts:
- changing Nora into an owner whose direct self-benefit may trigger a main-purpose path
- changing the promise so Beacon looks only to Nora from the outset
- changing who owes the underlying debt
- using broader wording that hides whether the promise ran to the creditor

Rubric focus points:
- correctly identifies creditor, debtor, and third-party promisor roles
- distinguishes collateral from original liability
- centers the missing writing
- avoids inventing a main-purpose exception that the source materials do not support
