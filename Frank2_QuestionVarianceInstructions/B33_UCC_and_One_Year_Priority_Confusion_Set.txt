UCC + ONE-YEAR — PRIORITY CONFUSION SET

Purpose:
This file helps the GPT handle oral sale-of-goods agreements that will be performed over more than one year, where a weak answer might jump to the common-law one-year provision instead of first asking whether Article 2 controls.

Why this confusion case matters:
Long duration can bait models into common-law one-year analysis even when the transaction is clearly a sale of goods.
That routing move is often wrong or at least incomplete.

Primary routing lesson:
When the agreement is clearly for the sale of goods, route to UCC Article 2 / Section 2-201 first.
Do not let the common-law one-year pack displace the governing sales framework unless the source materials expressly require that extra step.

Competing route map:
Candidate route A:
UCC Article 2 / Section 2-201

Candidate route B:
Common-law one-year provision

Relationship between routes:
Priority.
Once Article 2 clearly governs, UCC 2-201 is the primary SoF route on this benchmark.

Benchmark packet

Source-style fact core:
Northline Office Supply orally agrees to sell 2,400 identical office chairs to Horizon Workspace at $300 each. The chairs are to be delivered in 24 monthly installments over two years. No signed writing memorializes the agreement. Horizon later refuses to proceed, arguing the contract is unenforceable because it could not be performed within one year.

Neutral legal question:
Northline Office Supply orally agrees to sell 2,400 identical office chairs to Horizon Workspace at $300 each, to be delivered in 24 monthly installments over two years. There is no signed writing. Horizon later argues that the agreement is unenforceable because it could not be performed within one year. Is the agreement enforceable?

Gold routing + benchmark answer:

Jurisdiction / Governing-law assumption
Assume baseline UCC Article 2 / Section 2-201 principles and no special jurisdiction-specific variation unless the source materials provide one.

Bottom-line routing outcome
The primary route is UCC 2-201, not the common-law one-year provision. The agreement is plainly for the sale of goods. The fact that delivery extends over more than one year does not justify skipping the Article 2 classification step.

Controlling doctrine
The controlling classification question is whether the transaction is governed by Article 2. On these facts, it is. That makes UCC 2-201 the proper first writing-analysis route.

Competing route analysis
UCC route:
The contract is for 2,400 identical office chairs, which are goods, at a total price far above the ordinary UCC threshold.

One-year route:
A weak answer may focus on the two-year delivery schedule and jump directly to the common-law one-year provision. But on this benchmark, that is not the correct first move because Article 2 governs the transaction type.

Relationship between the routes
This is a priority problem. The existence of a long performance schedule does not displace the governing UCC framework. If the source materials later layer in another route, they may do so, but the benchmark first route is UCC 2-201.

Main gate order
classify as sale of goods -> confirm threshold -> assess writing, quantity, and authentication -> assess any supported UCC substitute -> only then address any source-specific residual issue

Transaction characterization
This is not a services contract and not a general long-term business relationship. It is a sale of identified goods to be delivered over time.

Writing / compliance analysis
The facts state that no signed writing memorializes the agreement. On the benchmark path, the ordinary UCC writing route is therefore missing, and the analysis should focus on whether any supported UCC substitute exists. The benchmark facts do not provide one.

Other possible routes and why they do or do not control
The one-year provision is not the primary route because the transaction is governed first by Article 2. The benchmark should not import generic common-law timing analysis unless the assigned source materials expressly do so.

Strongest counterargument
Northline's strongest argument would be any available UCC-specific substitute route. On the stated facts, though, no merchant confirmation, admission, or payment/acceptance facts are supplied.

Bounded uncertainty
The route would become less stable if the transaction were mixed goods and services rather than clearly goods, or if the source materials explicitly layered a separate timing rule on top of Article 2. On the stated facts, Article 2 classification is clear.

Carry-forward routing notes:
Core answer reuse:
- Cosmetic edits only if the contract remains clearly a sale of goods and the no-writing status remains fixed.

Safest Lane A zones after routing is fixed:
- names
- product labels that remain goods
- neutral price or quantity shifts that stay on the same threshold side
- duration changes that preserve the same installment-over-time structure without changing goods classification

Best Lane B ambiguity targets:
- whether the transaction is predominantly goods or services
- whether the writing contains quantity
- whether any merchant confirmation or other UCC substitute exists
- whether the delivery schedule is fixed or merely projected

Unsafe drifts:
- changing the deal into a consulting or maintenance agreement
- removing quantity and pretending the UCC path is still fully stable
- treating any over-one-year goods contract as automatically controlled by the common-law one-year pack

Rubric focus points:
A strong answer should:
- classify the transaction under Article 2 before discussing duration
- explain why long duration alone does not displace UCC routing
- keep the writing analysis UCC-specific
- avoid importing one-year doctrine language as the primary rule

That is a major source of error in mixed or long-duration sales problems.
