WORKED BENCHMARK ANSWER

Jurisdiction assumption:
U.S. common law / Restatement-style framing.

Bottom-line outcome:
The oral promise is likely not enforceable.

Controlling doctrine:
The likely controlling doctrine is the Statute of Frauds category for a contract made upon consideration of marriage.

Transaction / formation characterization:
There is a plausible bargain framing because the father promised to pay the son’s student loans if the son married a specific person within the stated period. The son’s prior intent to propose does not necessarily defeat bargain formation by itself, but it does weaken later inducement and reliance arguments. Even assuming there was a bargain rather than a conditional gift, enforceability still fails at the main Statute of Frauds gate.

Writing requirement and trigger:
The marriage-consideration gate is the likely dispositive issue. A promise made in exchange for marriage, other than the mutual promises between the spouses themselves, ordinarily falls within the Statute of Frauds and requires a signed writing. Here, the father’s promise was tied to the son’s marrying a specific person, so marriage was the consideration for the promise. Because there is no signed writing, that gate strongly supports nonenforcement.

The son’s completed marriage does not automatically remove this category from the Statute of Frauds. Full performance by carrying out the requested marriage is not, by itself, enough to eliminate the writing requirement for this category.

The one-year provision is not the main bar here. Even though the condition window was eighteen months and the marriage in fact occurred after fourteen months, the correct question is whether performance was possible within one year at the time of formation. Because the marriage could have occurred within one year, the one-year provision is not triggered.

Suretyship is a secondary and more nuanced issue. The father promised the son that he would pay the son’s student loans, but classic suretyship analysis typically turns on a promise to a creditor to answer for another’s debt. On these facts, the promise is to the son rather than to the lender, which weakens or may defeat the classic suretyship trigger. Even if a suretyship issue were present, it would not cure the separate and independent marriage-consideration Statute of Frauds problem.

Compliance / substitute / exception analysis:
Any fallback doctrine should be analyzed only after the main gates. If a suretyship writing requirement were actually triggered, a main-purpose theory might be argued on the ground that the father sought a personal economic benefit through a hoped-for tax deduction. But that argument would matter only to suretyship and would not solve the separate marriage-consideration writing problem.

Promissory estoppel is a possible but weak fallback. The main problem is inducement and reliance: the son already planned to propose, which weakens the claim that the father’s promise caused the marriage. In addition, many jurisdictions are cautious about using estoppel to override Statute of Frauds categories, especially where the category is designed to require formal proof. Even where estoppel is available, the remedy may be limited rather than giving full enforcement. On these facts, promissory estoppel is not a strong path to enforcement.

Other defenses or competing doctrines:
The father’s later realization that he would not receive the expected tax deduction is generally a unilateral mistake about an anticipated benefit, not an automatic excuse. Unless the promise was expressly conditioned on actually receiving the deduction, that mistake does not discharge the duty to perform. But this point matters only if the promise would otherwise be enforceable, which it likely is not.

Strongest counterargument:
The strongest counterargument is that the promise should be enforced through a fallback theory because the father had a concrete economic motive, the son completed the requested marriage, and fairness may seem to favor enforcement after the son changed position. But that argument is still weaker than the main Statute of Frauds analysis because the marriage-consideration gate is the likely controller, and the son’s prior intent to marry weakens the reliance story. The main uncertainty is jurisdiction sensitivity at the margins: some jurisdictions may frame the marriage-consideration category differently, treat estoppel more generously, or analyze debt-payment promises in a more expansive suretyship framework. But on the stated facts, the strongest analysis is that the oral promise is not enforceable because the marriage-consideration Statute of Frauds gate controls and no signed writing exists.
