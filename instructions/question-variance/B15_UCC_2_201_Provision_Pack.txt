STATUTE OF FRAUDS PACK — UCC ARTICLE 2 / SECTION 2-201

Purpose:
This pack helps the GPT handle Article 2 Statute of Frauds problems by keeping the analysis centered on sale-of-goods classification, threshold, writing sufficiency, and the specific substitute routes that the benchmark actually uses.

Scope statement:
Use this pack when the likely controlling issue is whether an agreement for the sale of goods is enforceable under UCC 2-201 despite missing or disputed writing compliance.

Baseline source-of-truth rule:
The benchmark answer and source materials control.
This pack assumes a baseline Article 2 / 2-201 framework and should be adapted to the assigned jurisdiction or source text.
Do not import merchant-confirmation, specially manufactured goods, admission, payment, receipt, or acceptance routes unless the source materials support them.

Use this pack when:
- the transaction is a sale of goods
- the threshold side matters under the selected source path
- the writing or quantity-term issue is central
- the problem may involve merchant confirmation or another 2-201 substitute route
- a mixed goods/services problem has already been classified into Article 2

Do not use this pack when:
- the transaction is mainly services or real property
- the classification between goods and services is still the main unresolved gate
- the benchmark does not actually rely on 2-201
- the alleged agreement concerns something that the source materials do not treat as goods

Default gate order:
1. determine whether Article 2 governs the transaction
2. determine whether the agreement is a sale of goods and whether the selected threshold is crossed
3. determine whether there is a writing sufficient under the benchmark path, including any required quantity or authentication feature
4. evaluate any supported merchant-confirmation route
5. evaluate any supported special-manufacture, admission, payment, receipt, or acceptance route
6. identify bounded uncertainty if goods classification, threshold, merchant status, quantity, or substitute-route facts are unclear

Anchor facts that must stay fixed in Lane A:
- goods versus services classification
- the side of the Article 2 threshold
- whether a writing exists
- whether the writing includes the feature the benchmark treats as essential, often quantity
- merchant status if merchant confirmation matters
- whether payment, receipt, acceptance, admission, or special-manufacture facts are present if the benchmark relies on them

Lane A safe variation zones:
1. goods-label swaps
Examples:
- “custom chairs” -> “custom desks”
- “industrial bolts” -> “industrial fasteners”
Use only if the items remain goods of the same legal type.

2. threshold-preserving numeric shifts
Examples:
- price changes or quantity changes that stay on the same side of the selected threshold and do not alter the writing logic
Use only if the quantity-term analysis remains the same.

3. role-preserving commercial-shell changes
Examples:
- names
- industry setting
- buyer/seller labels
Use only if merchant status and transaction type remain fixed.

4. non-controlling salience injections
Examples:
- famous retailer
- luxury branding
- vivid shipping context
Use only if the added fact does not create a new exception or classification issue.

Lane B ambiguity targets:
1. predominant-purpose ambiguity
Old:
“The contract was for the sale of 1,000 units of equipment plus incidental installation.”
New:
“The contract was for a commercial package including equipment and related work.”
Why this matters:
The answer should identify that the goods/services classification may control whether Article 2 applies.

2. quantity-term ambiguity
Old:
“The signed writing stated 1,000 units.”
New:
“The signed writing stated the parties’ agreement for the equipment.”
Why this matters:
The answer should identify whether the writing contains the feature the benchmark treats as essential.

3. merchant-status ambiguity
Old:
“Both parties were merchants in these goods.”
New:
“Both parties did business in the industry.”
Why this matters:
The answer should identify whether the merchant-confirmation route is really available.

4. substitute-route ambiguity
Old:
“The buyer received and accepted half the goods.”
New:
“The transaction partially moved forward.”
Why this matters:
The answer should identify which concrete facts would matter under the chosen substitute route.

Unsafe variation patterns:
- converting goods into services or vice versa without labeling the shift
- changing the transaction from below threshold to above threshold or the reverse in a supposed Lane A package
- removing the quantity feature from the writing but still claiming answer invariance
- changing merchant status when merchant confirmation is central
- importing common-law SoF reasoning when the benchmark is clearly Article 2 centered

Recommended default menu seeds for B02:
Option 1 — Lane A / Goods-label swap
What changes:
Product label, industry context, and party shell only.
Expected answer reuse:
Cosmetic edits only.
Main red flag:
Do not change goods classification.

Option 2 — Lane A / Threshold-preserving commercial detail shift
What changes:
Price, quantity, or sales context while keeping threshold side and writing logic fixed.
Expected answer reuse:
Reuse as-is or Cosmetic edits only.
Main red flag:
Do not change the quantity-term path.

Option 3 — Lane B / Quantity-term ambiguity
What changes:
Generalizes what the writing says so the model must identify the missing essential feature.
Expected answer reuse:
Ambiguity rewrite required.
Main red flag:
Do not quietly restore the quantity fact.

Option 4 — Lane B / Merchant or substitute-route ambiguity
What changes:
Blurs merchant status, confirmation facts, or acceptance/payment facts.
Expected answer reuse:
Ambiguity rewrite required.
Main red flag:
Do not blur multiple substitute routes at once unless the point is that the route is unstable.

Worked micro examples:
Safe Lane A:
Old:
“A buyer orally agreed to purchase 800 industrial pumps from a seller. The only signed writing omitted the disputed legal feature under the benchmark path.”
New:
“A buyer orally agreed to purchase 800 industrial valves from a seller. The only signed writing omitted the disputed legal feature under the benchmark path.”
Why usually safe:
The transaction remains a sale of goods and the writing defect remains the same.

Safe Lane B:
Old:
“The signed memorandum stated 800 pumps.”
New:
“The signed memorandum referred to the equipment order.”
Why this works:
The model should identify that the content of the writing matters and may not be sufficient under the benchmark path.

Unsafe:
Old:
“The contract was for the sale of 800 pumps.”
New:
“The contract was for design, installation, and maintenance services involving pumps.”
Why unsafe:
The governing-law route may shift away from Article 2.

Answer-shape emphasis:
Jurisdiction / Governing-law assumption:
State clearly that the analysis is under Article 2 if classification is stable.

Bottom-line outcome:
State whether the agreement likely satisfies or fails the 2-201 path used by the benchmark.

Controlling doctrine:
Name the key gate as Article 2 classification, threshold, writing sufficiency, or the substitute route actually in play.

Transaction characterization:
Identify the goods, the buyer-seller relationship, and any mixed-contract issue.

Trigger analysis:
Explain why 2-201 applies or why a missing classification fact makes that uncertain.

Writing / compliance analysis:
Focus on the exact writing feature the benchmark treats as important.
Do not drift into generic common-law memorandum language if the benchmark is UCC-specific.

Exception / substitute analysis:
Limit yourself to the specific 2-201 route the benchmark supports:
- merchant confirmation
- specially manufactured goods
- admission
- payment
- receipt or acceptance
or another supported route

Strongest counterargument:
Usually that Article 2 does not govern, that the writing is sufficient, or that a supported substitute route makes the agreement enforceable.

Bounded uncertainty:
If goods classification, quantity, merchant status, or substitute facts are missing, say so directly.

Rubric focus points:
A strong answer should:
- classify the transaction into Article 2 before applying 2-201
- identify whether the threshold is crossed under the selected source path
- tie writing sufficiency to the actual benchmark feature at issue
- analyze only the substitute routes genuinely supported by the facts
- avoid importing common-law SoF logic when the benchmark is UCC-centered

Main red flags:
- the answer never decides whether Article 2 applies
- the variation changes merchant status without saying so
- the updated answer assumes a quantity or other writing feature after a Lane B omission removed it
- the model treats any partial performance fact as automatically enough without the benchmark path
- the variation silently turns a goods contract into a mixed or services contract

Status rule:
Mark the package Ready only if the agreement remains in the same Article 2 / 2-201 lane in Lane A, or if the Lane B version isolates one missing classification, writing, merchant, or substitute-route fact.
Otherwise mark Needs targeted revision or Unsafe.
