STATUTE OF FRAUDS PACK — SURETYSHIP PROVISION

Purpose:
This pack helps the GPT handle promises to answer for another person’s debt or default without collapsing original undertakings, indemnity-like promises, and ordinary guaranties into one undifferentiated category.

Scope statement:
Use this pack when the likely controlling issue is whether a promise is collateral — a promise to answer for the debt or default of another — and therefore requires a writing under the governing source path.
Do not use this pack merely because three parties appear in the fact pattern.

Baseline source-of-truth rule:
The benchmark answer and source materials control.
This pack is only a baseline guide.
Different sources handle original undertaking, leading-object or main-purpose reasoning, and related doctrines differently.
Do not import a specific exception unless the source materials support it.

Use this pack when:
- the facts suggest a guaranty or backup-payment promise
- one person allegedly promises a creditor that another person’s debt will be paid
- the key dispute is collateral versus original liability
- the source materials treat self-benefit or leading-object reasoning as part of the doctrinal path

Do not use this pack when:
- the promisor appears to be taking on primary liability from the outset
- the dispute is mainly formation, consideration, agency, or another doctrine with no real suretyship gate
- the parties and debt structure are too unclear to tell whether the promise is collateral

Default gate order:
1. identify the underlying obligor, the creditor, and the alleged surety
2. determine whether the promise is collateral or original
3. determine whether a writing is required and whether one exists
4. evaluate any main-purpose, self-benefit, or equivalent route only if the source materials support it
5. identify bounded uncertainty if the promise direction, addressee, or liability structure is unclear

Anchor facts that must stay fixed in Lane A:
- who owes the underlying debt
- who allegedly promised to answer for that debt
- whether the promise is collateral or original under the benchmark
- who received the promise if that matters to the source path
- facts supporting or defeating a self-benefit or leading-object theory
- whether any writing exists

Lane A safe variation zones:
1. business-context swaps
Examples:
- “apartment rent debt” -> “inventory financing debt”
- “friend’s loan” -> “supplier invoice”
Use only if the liability structure is unchanged.

2. role-preserving shell changes
Examples:
- names
- industries
- business sizes
- neutral debt labels

3. salience injections
Examples:
- large debt amount
- famous bank
- high-status promisor
Use only if the amount or identity does not create a new threshold or bias problem in the benchmark.

Lane B ambiguity targets:
1. collateral-versus-original ambiguity
Old:
“If D does not pay, I will pay you.”
New:
“I will see that this gets paid.”
Why this matters:
The new language may no longer clearly reveal whether the promisor undertook secondary or primary liability.

2. addressee ambiguity
Old:
“The promisor told the creditor, ‘If D does not pay, I will.’”
New:
“The promisor said the debt would be taken care of.”
Why this matters:
The answer may now need to identify to whom the promise was made.

3. self-benefit ambiguity
Old:
“The promisor guaranteed the debt to keep the supplier delivering to the promisor’s own business.”
New:
“The promisor guaranteed the debt in connection with the business relationship.”
Why this matters:
The self-benefit route may now be uncertain.

4. obligor-liability ambiguity
Old:
“The original debtor remained liable.”
New:
“The parties treated the obligation as covered.”
Why this matters:
The answer may need to identify whether the transaction was really a novation or an original undertaking.

Unsafe variation patterns:
- changing who owes the underlying debt
- converting a backup promise into a primary obligation without labeling the shift
- changing the promisee from creditor to debtor when the source path depends on that distinction
- adding facts that resolve the main-purpose issue in the opposite direction
- using vague language that hides both the addressee and the liability structure at once

Recommended default menu seeds for B02:
Option 1 — Lane A / Role-preserving business-context swap
What changes:
Industry, debt label, party names, and setting only.
Expected answer reuse:
Cosmetic edits only.
Main red flag:
Keep liability direction fixed.

Option 2 — Lane A / Salience injection
What changes:
Adds vivid but non-controlling details such as a large amount or famous lender.
Expected answer reuse:
Reuse as-is or Cosmetic edits only.
Main red flag:
Do not change any self-benefit or main-purpose facts.

Option 3 — Lane B / Original-vs-collateral ambiguity
What changes:
Blurs whether the promisor undertook secondary or primary liability.
Expected answer reuse:
Ambiguity rewrite required.
Main red flag:
Do not blur every role at once.

Option 4 — Lane B / Main-purpose ambiguity
What changes:
Generalizes the promisor’s reason for making the promise.
Expected answer reuse:
Ambiguity rewrite required.
Main red flag:
Do not silently reverse the existing exception analysis.

Worked micro examples:
Safe Lane A:
Old:
“A promised the bank that if B defaulted on the equipment loan, A would pay. There was no writing.”
New:
“A logistics executive promised the supplier that if B defaulted on the inventory financing debt, A would pay. There was no writing.”
Why usually safe:
The creditor-debtor-surety structure and lack of writing remain unchanged.

Safe Lane B:
Old:
“A told the bank, ‘If B does not pay, I will.’”
New:
“A said the bank would be paid.”
Why this works:
The model should identify that it is unclear whether A made a collateral promise, undertook primary liability, or even addressed the statement to the bank.

Unsafe:
Old:
“A told the bank, ‘If B does not pay, I will.’”
New:
“A agreed to buy the equipment directly and pay the bank.”
Why unsafe:
The new facts may create original liability rather than suretyship.

Answer-shape emphasis:
Jurisdiction / Governing-law assumption:
Usually common law unless the source materials say otherwise.

Bottom-line outcome:
State whether the promise is likely within the suretyship provision and whether writing is required.

Controlling doctrine:
Name the key gate: collateral versus original liability.

Transaction characterization:
Identify creditor, obligor, promisor, and the direction of the promise.

Trigger analysis:
Explain why the promise does or does not answer for another’s debt.

Writing / compliance analysis:
Tie the writing question to the correct liability characterization.

Exception / substitute analysis:
Only analyze main-purpose, self-benefit, or equivalent routes if the source materials genuinely use them.

Strongest counterargument:
Usually that the promisor undertook an original obligation, or that a writing or equivalent substitute exists.

Bounded uncertainty:
If it is unclear whether the promise was collateral, say exactly that and explain which missing fact controls.

Rubric focus points:
A strong answer should:
- identify the underlying debt and role structure
- distinguish collateral from original liability
- keep the writing analysis downstream from that classification
- use any self-benefit or main-purpose reasoning only if supported
- avoid blurring addressee, liability direction, and obligor status

Main red flags:
- every three-party promise is labeled suretyship without classification
- the answer ignores whether the original debtor remained liable
- the updated answer assumes facts removed by a Lane B omission
- the variation quietly changes creditor/debtor direction
- the answer imports guaranty doctrine language not grounded in the benchmark

Status rule:
Mark the package Ready only if the liability structure remains stable in Lane A, or if the Lane B version isolates one missing classification fact without dissolving the whole debt structure.
Otherwise mark Needs targeted revision or Unsafe.
