Benchmark packet

Source-style fact core:
As administrator of her aunt's estate, Tessa signs an email to a funeral home stating, "If the estate account falls short, I will pay the remaining balance from my own funds." The funeral home proceeds with the services. A dispute later arises over whether Tessa is personally bound.

Neutral legal question:
As administrator of her aunt's estate, Tessa signs an email to a funeral home stating, "If the estate account falls short, I will pay the remaining balance from my own funds." The funeral home proceeds with the services. Is Tessa's promise within the executor personal-promise provision, and does the email matter?

Gold benchmark answer:

Jurisdiction / Governing-law assumption
Assume ordinary executor or administrator Statute of Frauds principles and no probate-specific variation beyond what the benchmark source materials support.

Bottom-line outcome
Likely enforceable against Tessa on the stated benchmark path. The promise is best read as a personal undertaking by a fiduciary to answer for an estate obligation, and the signed email likely satisfies the writing requirement.

Controlling doctrine
The controlling doctrine is the executor or administrator personal-promise provision of the Statute of Frauds: a fiduciary's personal promise to answer for an estate obligation generally requires a qualifying writing.

Main gate order
identify the fiduciary role and estate obligation -> decide whether the promise is personal or representative -> assess writing -> assess any supported substitute only if needed

Transaction characterization
Tessa is not an ordinary third-party guarantor. She is the estate's administrator. The threshold characterization issue is whether she promised merely that the estate would pay, or whether she personally undertook the obligation if estate funds were insufficient.

Trigger analysis
The promise appears to be a personal undertaking because Tessa says she will pay the remaining balance from her own funds. That language distinguishes the promise from a routine representative statement about estate administration.

Writing / compliance analysis
The promise appears in a signed email attributable to Tessa. On a baseline benchmark path, that writing likely satisfies the statute because it memorializes the personal nature of the undertaking and identifies the estate-related obligation.

Exception / substitute / enforcement-limitation analysis
No substitute route is needed because the benchmark can proceed through the writing path. The benchmark should not import unrelated probate doctrines or generic suretyship exceptions unless the source materials specifically do so.

Other defenses or competing doctrines
The main competing argument is representative capacity, not a separate body of probate law. The benchmark should stay focused on whether Tessa personally bound herself.

Strongest counterargument
Tessa's best counterargument is that she was communicating only in her administrative role and merely describing how the estate matter would be handled. But the explicit promise to pay from her own funds makes the stronger baseline reading a personal undertaking.

Bounded uncertainty
The answer would be less stable if the email did not mention Tessa's own funds, if the statement were made only internally rather than to the creditor, or if the message were unsigned or unattributable. On the stated facts, the key capacity signal is clear.

Unsafe drifts:

- shifting the debt from an estate obligation to Tessa's own obligation
- collapsing executor-capacity analysis into generic suretyship without marking the doctrinal change
