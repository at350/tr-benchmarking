STATUTE OF FRAUDS PACK — LAND SALE / INTEREST IN LAND PROVISION

Purpose:
This pack helps the GPT handle land-related Statute of Frauds problems by forcing a careful characterization of the transaction and the land interest before any writing or exception analysis begins.

Scope statement:
Use this pack when the likely controlling issue is whether the agreement transfers, creates, or materially affects an interest in land and therefore requires a writing under the governing source path.

Baseline source-of-truth rule:
The benchmark answer and source materials control.
Land-related Statute of Frauds doctrine varies across sources, especially on part performance, estoppel, leases, and memorandum sufficiency.
Do not import a land exception or essential-term rule unless the benchmark path supports it.

Use this pack when:
- the agreement is for the sale of land
- the agreement creates or transfers another covered interest in land under the source path
- the key gate is whether the transaction is one for an interest in land
- the benchmark focuses on writing sufficiency, property description, or part performance

Do not use this pack when:
- the transaction is really a services agreement related to real estate
- the issue is a goods or financing transaction merely connected to land
- the source materials do not treat the alleged interest as a covered land interest
- classification between land and non-land is still the main unresolved gate

Default gate order:
1. characterize the transaction and the asserted interest in land
2. determine whether that type of land transaction triggers a writing requirement under the benchmark path
3. evaluate whether a writing or memorandum exists and whether it is sufficient under the selected source path
4. evaluate only the supported part-performance, estoppel, or equivalent route if the benchmark uses it
5. identify bounded uncertainty if the nature of the interest, the writing status, or the exception facts are unclear

Anchor facts that must stay fixed in Lane A:
- whether the transaction concerns an interest in land at all
- the type of interest at issue
- whether a writing exists
- property-description adequacy if the benchmark makes it relevant
- possession, payment, and improvement facts if they drive exception analysis
- whether the benchmark already uses part performance, estoppel, or another substitute

Lane A safe variation zones:
1. parcel-shell changes
Examples:
- street address changes
- county changes
- lot labels
Use only if jurisdiction does not change and the land-interest function remains identical.

2. price or asset-salience changes
Examples:
- purchase price changes that do not create another doctrinal issue
- adding scenic or luxury descriptors
Use only if the price or description does not change the legal path.

3. party and setting changes
Examples:
- names
- occupations
- neutral local context
Use only if the transaction remains the same type of land-interest agreement.

Lane B ambiguity targets:
1. land-interest ambiguity
Old:
“A agreed to sell Blackacre to B.”
New:
“A agreed to transfer rights connected to the property to B.”
Why this matters:
The answer may now need to identify whether the agreement actually involves a covered interest in land.

2. memorandum ambiguity
Old:
“There was no signed writing.”
New:
“The parties discussed the deal informally and exchanged communications.”
Why this matters:
The answer should identify that writing existence and sufficiency matter.

3. part-performance ambiguity
Old:
“B paid part of the price, took possession, and made improvements.”
New:
“B acted in reliance on the agreement.”
Why this matters:
The answer should identify which concrete facts matter under the exception path.

4. property-description ambiguity
Old:
“The memorandum described Lot 7 of Greenacre Estates.”
New:
“The memorandum described the seller’s property.”
Why this matters:
The sufficiency of the writing may now be uncertain.

Unsafe variation patterns:
- converting a land-transfer agreement into a brokerage, construction, or services agreement without labeling the doctrinal shift
- deleting all land-interest details so the route itself disappears
- adding possession, payment, or improvement facts that change the exception posture
- changing the adequacy of the writing while still labeling the package answer-invariant
- treating any property-related discussion as a covered land contract

Recommended default menu seeds for B02:
Option 1 — Lane A / Parcel-shell swap
What changes:
Address, lot description, local setting, and party names only.
Expected answer reuse:
Cosmetic edits only.
Main red flag:
Do not change the land-interest type.

Option 2 — Lane A / Non-controlling salience injection
What changes:
Price detail, luxury descriptor, or local color that should not affect doctrine.
Expected answer reuse:
Reuse as-is or Cosmetic edits only.
Main red flag:
Do not create a new exception or financing issue.

Option 3 — Lane B / Part-performance ambiguity
What changes:
Generalizes concrete reliance facts such as possession, payment, or improvements.
Expected answer reuse:
Ambiguity rewrite required.
Main red flag:
Do not erase every exception-related fact at once.

Option 4 — Lane B / Writing-sufficiency ambiguity
What changes:
Blurs whether a signed memorandum exists or whether the description is adequate.
Expected answer reuse:
Ambiguity rewrite required.
Main red flag:
Do not quietly assume a sufficient writing in the updated answer.

Worked micro examples:
Safe Lane A:
Old:
“A orally agreed to sell B a parcel in Harris County for $300,000. No signed writing existed.”
New:
“A orally agreed to sell B a lot in Travis County for $325,000. No signed writing existed.”
Why usually safe:
The transaction remains a land sale and the writing status is unchanged.

Safe Lane B:
Old:
“B paid part of the purchase price, took possession, and built a fence.”
New:
“B relied on the agreement and changed position.”
Why this works:
The model should identify that the specific reliance facts matter to any part-performance route.

Unsafe:
Old:
“A orally agreed to sell B land.”
New:
“A orally agreed to build a guesthouse on B’s land.”
Why unsafe:
The new facts may shift the problem from a land-transfer agreement to a services contract.

Answer-shape emphasis:
Jurisdiction / Governing-law assumption:
Briefly state the land-interest framework only as needed.

Bottom-line outcome:
State whether the agreement likely falls within the land provision and whether the writing or supported exception analysis controls.

Controlling doctrine:
Name the key gate as “covered interest in land” if classification is disputed.

Transaction characterization:
Describe the exact land-related interest and why it does or does not fall within the selected source path.

Trigger analysis:
Explain why the transaction counts as a land-interest agreement or why that classification is uncertain.

Writing / compliance analysis:
Focus on memorandum existence and whatever essential writing features the benchmark uses.

Exception / substitute analysis:
Stay tightly bounded.
Only discuss part performance, estoppel, or equivalent routes if the source materials support them.

Strongest counterargument:
Usually that the transaction is not actually a covered land-interest agreement, or that the writing or exception route is sufficient.

Bounded uncertainty:
If the interest type, writing sufficiency, or reliance facts are missing, say exactly which fact matters.

Rubric focus points:
A strong answer should:
- characterize the transaction before jumping to exceptions
- explain whether the asserted interest is one the selected source path covers
- keep writing sufficiency tied to the actual benchmark
- identify concrete exception facts rather than vague reliance language
- avoid importing land doctrines not present in the source materials

Main red flags:
- the answer never identifies the specific land interest
- the variation changes part-performance facts but still claims answer invariance
- the updated answer reintroduces possession or improvement facts that a Lane B version removed
- the model assumes any email or communication is a sufficient memorandum without benchmark support
- the variation turns a land issue into a services issue without saying so

Status rule:
Mark the package Ready only if the agreement remains in the same land-interest lane in Lane A, or if the Lane B version isolates one missing land-trigger, writing, or exception fact.
Otherwise mark Needs targeted revision or Unsafe.
