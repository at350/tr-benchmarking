Benchmark packet

Source-style fact core:
Metro Restaurant Supply orally agrees to sell 200 stainless-steel mixing bowls to Harbor Bakery for $15 each. Both parties are merchants. The next day, Metro sends Harbor an email confirming the agreement, signed with the sales manager's typed business signature. Harbor receives the email and does not object in writing within ten days. Harbor later refuses to perform.

Neutral legal question:
Metro Restaurant Supply orally agrees to sell 200 stainless-steel mixing bowls to Harbor Bakery for $15 each. The next day, Metro sends Harbor an email confirming the agreement, signed with the sales manager's typed business signature. Harbor receives the email and does not object in writing within ten days. Harbor later refuses to perform. Is the agreement enforceable under UCC 2-201?

Gold benchmark answer:

Jurisdiction / Governing-law assumption
Assume baseline UCC Article 2 and Section 2-201 rules, and no jurisdiction-specific variation beyond what the benchmark source materials support.

Bottom-line outcome
Likely enforceable under UCC 2-201 on the stated facts. This is a sale-of-goods contract above the threshold, but the merchant-confirmation route likely satisfies the Statute of Frauds.

Controlling doctrine
The controlling doctrine is UCC Section 2-201. The key path is merchant confirmation: a writing sufficient against the sender that is received by another merchant and not timely objected to in writing.

Main gate order
determine Article 2 applies -> confirm threshold is met -> assess writing, quantity term, and authentication -> assess merchant confirmation

Transaction characterization
The contract is for the sale of 200 stainless-steel mixing bowls, which are goods. Nothing in the problem suggests a mixed services issue.

Trigger analysis
The transaction is a sale of goods above the baseline Article 2 threshold. That brings Section 2-201 into play.

Writing / compliance analysis
Metro's email appears to contain a quantity term, identify the transaction, and include an authentication feature through the sales manager's typed business signature. On the benchmark path, that makes the writing likely sufficient against Metro as sender.

Exception / substitute / enforcement-limitation analysis
Because both sides are merchants, Harbor received the confirmation, and Harbor did not object in writing within ten days, the merchant-confirmation route likely makes the agreement enforceable against Harbor as well. The benchmark does not need to rely on specially manufactured goods, admission, or payment/acceptance routes.

Other defenses or competing doctrines
No separate common-law Statute of Frauds analysis is needed because Article 2 is the governing framework on these facts.

Strongest counterargument
Harbor's best counterargument is that the email is too informal or that the typed signature is insufficient. But the stronger baseline answer is that the confirmation is attributable to Metro and contains the key quantity information needed for the merchant-confirmation route.

Bounded uncertainty
The answer would be less stable if merchant status were unclear, if the email omitted quantity, or if Harbor had objected in writing within ten days. On the stated facts, those control points are fixed.
