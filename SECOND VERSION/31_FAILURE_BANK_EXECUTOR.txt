31_FAILURE_BANK_EXECUTOR

PACK 30 — FAILURE BANK — EXECUTOR / ADMINISTRATOR PERSONAL PROMISE

Purpose
This file is the pack-specific failure bank for Statute of Frauds problems involving a personal representative's special promise to answer a decedent or estate debt out of the representative's own estate. It should be used with 30_DOCTRINE_PACK_EXECUTOR.txt and the global core files.

Use 06_CORE_SELF_AUDIT.txt for generic problems such as invented facts, missing headings, or failure to follow the required output shape. Use this file when the failure is specific to executor/administrator promise doctrine or Pack 30 routing.

This is a drafting and evaluation aid, not final legal authority. Outputs remain for JD review.

SCOPE / TRIGGER
Use Pack 30 when the controlling issue is whether an executor, administrator, or modern personal representative personally undertook to pay a debt or damages of the decedent or estate from the representative's own funds or estate.

Classic trigger:
"If the estate cannot pay, I will pay from my own money" or equivalent personal-payment language.

Core distinction:
A promise out of the representative's own estate is not the same as a promise only to pay from estate assets.

Critical companion distinction:
A debt of the decedent or estate is not the same as an obligation newly incurred by the representative in the course of administration.

Do not use this bank as the primary bank when:
- the promise is only that the estate will pay from estate assets
- the obligation is the representative's own original obligation incurred during administration
- the real issue is ordinary suretyship for a living debtor
- the real issue is land, UCC § 2-201, marriage consideration, or the one-year provision
- the problem is general probate procedure, fiduciary duty, or claim allowance without a real personal-promise issue

If Pack 30 fit remains weak after source extraction, do not force a final benchmark answer. State the routing uncertainty and stop at source extraction plus JD review flags if needed.

PRIMARY LABELING RULE
Pick the earliest material failure.

Use one primary label. Add at most two secondary labels if they independently matter.

Ordering preference:
1. Routing / scope
2. Capacity and whose debt
3. Writing trigger
4. Writing satisfaction
5. Substitute / exception / fallback
6. Strongest counterargument / residual uncertainty

CORE DECISION LADDER
Gate 1. Is there actually a promise by a personal representative?
Gate 2. Is the underlying obligation a debt of the decedent or estate, or a new obligation of the representative incurred in administration?
Gate 3. Is the promise personal, out of the representative's own estate, or merely representative, out of estate assets?
Gate 4. If personal and collateral, does the executor/administrator clause trigger a writing requirement?
Gate 5. If triggered, is there a signed writing or memorandum that captures the essential commitment and enough capacity clarity?
Gate 6. If the writing is missing or weak, is there a source-supported fallback or a reason the promise is outside the clause?
Gate 7. What is the strongest counterargument and what remains uncertain?

PRIMARY FAILURE LABELS

E00 ROUTING / FIT

E00A Wrong-pack routing
Definition:
The answer routes the problem into Pack 30 even though another pack or doctrine is controlling.

Typical symptoms:
- answer centers land-sale writing rules, UCC merchant-confirmation rules, marriage consideration, or one-year analysis
- answer treats an ordinary guaranty by a non-representative as an executor problem
- answer treats generic probate administration as the main issue

Use when:
This error comes before any executor-specific analysis.

Do not use when:
Pack 30 is correct but the answer then misstates executor doctrine. In that situation, use E10 or E20 instead.

E00B No real personal-promise issue
Definition:
The answer treats a dispute as an executor-promise case even though the source only shows estate liability, claim procedure, or representative conduct, not a personal promise out of the representative's own estate.

Typical symptoms:
- "the estate will pay you" is treated as enough
- answer jumps to Statute of Frauds without identifying a personal undertaking
- answer confuses creditor-claim allowance with personal contractual liability

E10 CAPACITY AND DUTY CHARACTERIZATION

E10A Personal vs representative capacity not separated
Definition:
The answer does not distinguish a representative's personal undertaking from a statement made only in fiduciary or estate capacity.

Typical symptoms:
- "executor promised to pay" with no analysis of whose funds are committed
- assumes title alone answers personal liability
- skips the personal-versus-representative question and moves straight to enforceability

Why material:
This is usually the first doctrinal gate in Pack 30.

E10B Underlying duty misidentified
Definition:
The answer fails to separate a debt of the decedent or estate from the representative's own original obligation incurred in administration.

Typical symptoms:
- treats legal services, merchandise, broker fees, funeral arrangements, or similar administration obligations as though they were automatically decedent debts within the executor clause
- ignores that a representative may be personally liable on obligations the representative contracts during administration even if the estate may reimburse
- never asks who actually became obligated at formation

Use this label before any writing-sufficiency label if the answer never correctly identifies whose obligation is involved.

E10C Capacity language or signature form treated as automatically dispositive
Definition:
The answer assumes that "Executor," "Administrator," or "Personal Representative" in the promise or signature block automatically makes the obligation representative only, or automatically personal, without contextual analysis.

Typical symptoms:
- "signed as executor, therefore never personal"
- "used first person, therefore automatically personal"
- title language is given conclusive weight without examining the whole undertaking

Correct approach:
Analyze the whole undertaking, the source of payment, the surrounding facts, and whose debt is being answered.

E10D Executor clause collapsed into ordinary suretyship
Definition:
The answer analyzes only "debt of another" and never explains why the representative-promise clause does or does not control.

Typical symptoms:
- pure guaranty analysis with no attention to decedent or estate debt and representative capacity
- answer uses living-debtor suretyship vocabulary as though it fully resolves Pack 30

Note:
Overlap may exist, but Pack 30 still requires explicit capacity and own-estate analysis.

E20 WRITING TRIGGER

E20A False positive writing trigger
Definition:
The answer says a writing is required merely because an executor or administrator discusses paying an estate debt.

Typical symptoms:
- every representative promise is treated as within the statute
- no distinction between payment from estate assets and payment from the representative's own estate
- no distinction between decedent's debt and the representative's own administrative obligation

E20B False negative writing trigger
Definition:
The answer misses the writing requirement even though the promise, as characterized by the source, is a personal undertaking to answer a decedent or estate debt out of the representative's own estate.

Typical symptoms:
- "executor is just helping the estate, so no writing issue"
- ignores express personal-payment language
- ignores facts showing the promise is collateral and personal

E20C Asset / insolvency facts misused
Definition:
The answer treats estate assets or insolvency as magic words rather than as evidence bearing on whether the promise is really personal.

Typical symptoms:
- states insolvency is always a formal element
- treats the mere existence of assets as automatically defeating personal liability
- ignores no-asset facts that strongly suggest the promise is out of the representative's own estate

Correct approach:
Use asset facts to inform the capacity and source-of-payment analysis, not as a crude substitute for it.

E20D Exact promise and timing not pinned down
Definition:
The answer never identifies the promise actually made at formation and therefore cannot tell whether the clause is triggered.

Typical symptoms:
- describes later conduct instead of the undertaking when made
- merges post-hoc statements, probate filings, and oral assurances into one undifferentiated promise
- fails to separate promise content from later performance problems

E30 WRITING SATISFACTION / MEMORANDUM

E30A Memorandum analysis skipped
Definition:
Once the answer triggers the executor clause, it fails to analyze whether any signed writing or memorandum exists.

Typical symptoms:
- jumps directly from "writing required" to "unenforceable"
- or jumps from "some paper exists" to enforceability without analyzing it

E30B Writing, signature, or terms invented
Definition:
The answer hallucinates a signed writing, key term, or capacity statement not supported by the source.

Typical symptoms:
- invents a note, email, check memo, or probate filing
- invents signature authority or a memorialized personal undertaking

This is often a fatal source-discipline problem but is included here because it commonly appears at the writing gate.

E30C Essential commitment or capacity missing from writing analysis
Definition:
The answer discusses a writing but never asks whether the writing captures the personal undertaking, the debt being answered, or the source of payment with enough clarity.

Typical symptoms:
- any writing by the representative is treated as enough
- no attention to whether the memorandum reflects personal versus representative liability
- no attention to who must sign as the party to be charged

E30D Writing sufficiency confused with proof of the underlying transaction
Definition:
The answer mixes the statute-of-frauds writing question with separate proof issues such as whether the transfer was a gift or a loan.

Typical symptoms:
- "because the loan is proven, no writing is needed"
- "because gift intent is disputed, the statute applies"

Correct approach:
First determine whether the executor clause is triggered, then whether a sufficient writing exists, while keeping proof disputes separate.

E40 SUBSTITUTES / EXCEPTIONS / FALLBACKS / WRONG IMPORTS

E40A Wrong-pack doctrine imported
Definition:
The answer imports doctrinal machinery from another Statute of Frauds pack without source support.

Typical symptoms:
- land part performance used as though it governs
- UCC merchant-confirmation, specially manufactured goods, or payment/delivery rules inserted
- marriage-consideration or one-year analysis used as the central executor answer
- generic suretyship exceptions pasted in without explaining the executor-specific fit

E40B Original-undertaking carve-out missed
Definition:
The answer misses that some obligations contracted by the representative in the course of administration are the representative's own obligations and therefore are outside the executor clause even if they are proper estate charges or later reimbursable.

Typical symptoms:
- administrator hires counsel or services for the estate and the answer still treats the promise as a decedent-debt promise requiring writing
- answer never asks whether the representative, not the decedent, is the contracting party

E40C Equity or promissory-estoppel escape asserted without source support
Definition:
The answer uses reliance, injustice, estoppel, partial performance, or similar equitable language as an automatic way around the executor clause without tying it to the jurisdiction or source.

Typical symptoms:
- generic "promissory estoppel defeats statute of frauds"
- policy arguments substituted for doctrine

E40D Source-supported fallback omitted
Definition:
After concluding that the executor clause blocks direct contract enforcement, the answer ignores an alternative theory or procedural path that the source itself preserves or flags.

Possible examples:
- probate claim theory
- restitution, unjust enrichment, or reimbursement
- separate original-undertaking theory

Use sparingly.
Do not create fallback theories from thin air. This label is for omission of source-grounded alternatives, not for failing to invent them.

E40E Benefit analysis mishandled
Definition:
The answer treats benefit to the estate or benefit to the representative as automatically resolving the statute question.

Typical symptoms:
- "estate benefitted, so no writing issue"
- "representative benefitted, so writing automatically unnecessary"

Benefit may matter, but it does not replace capacity and source-of-payment analysis.

E50 COUNTERARGUMENTS / COMPETING ISSUES / UNCERTAINTY

E50A Strongest counterargument omitted
Definition:
The answer does not surface the best competing characterization.

Typical counterarguments in Pack 30:
- promise was only representative, not personal
- underlying obligation was the representative's own original undertaking
- there were estate assets, so payment was expected from the estate
- no sufficient writing memorialized a personal promise

E50B Separate issue conflated with Pack 30 doctrine
Definition:
The answer collapses the executor-clause issue into a different issue without keeping the doctrines separate.

Typical symptoms:
- loan-versus-gift proof dispute replaces the statute analysis
- one-year performance issue replaces the capacity and source-of-payment inquiry
- general probate claim-allowance rules replace the contract-enforceability issue

E50C Overconfident outcome despite source or jurisdiction uncertainty
Definition:
The answer states a categorical conclusion where the source leaves open key points such as capacity, estate solvency or assets, wording of the promise, or jurisdiction-specific treatment of fallback theories.

Typical symptoms:
- no specific uncertainty folded into "Strongest counterargument:" when the source is mixed
- unqualified statements that a promise always is or is not within the statute

OPTIONAL SECONDARY LABELS

S1 Mixed-capacity ambiguity underexplained
Use when the answer spots the issue but does not explain how conflicting indicators cut for and against personal liability.

S2 Own-estate language noticed but underused
Use when the answer mentions "out of my own money" or equivalent language but does not connect it cleanly to the writing trigger.

S3 Decedent-debt versus administration-debt distinction noticed but underdeveloped
Use when the answer recognizes the issue but never explains why it changes the executor-clause analysis.

S4 Representative title overweighted
Use when the answer gives too much weight to the representative title, but the core reasoning is otherwise sound.

MATERIALITY GUIDE

Usually fatal or primary:
- E00A, E00B
- E10A, E10B, E10D
- E20A, E20B

Usually major:
- E10C
- E20C, E20D
- E30A, E30B, E30C
- E40A, E40B

Usually secondary unless the source makes them central:
- E30D
- E40C, E40D, E40E
- E50A, E50B, E50C
- S1-S4

QUICK LABEL EXAMPLES

Example 1
Facts:
Executor says, "The estate will pay the old invoice when assets come in."
Bad answer:
"Because an executor promised to pay a decedent's debt, a signed writing is required."
Primary label:
E20A
Why:
The answer never distinguishes estate payment from payment out of the representative's own estate.

Example 2
Facts:
Administrator hires counsel during administration and orally promises payment.
Bad answer:
"This is an executor promise to answer the decedent's debt and is unenforceable unless written."
Primary label:
E10B
Secondary label:
E40B
Why:
The answer misidentifies whose obligation is involved before it ever reaches the writing gate.

Example 3
Facts:
Personal representative says, "If the estate cannot cover it, I will pay from my own funds."
Bad answer:
"No writing issue because the debt belongs to the estate."
Primary label:
E20B
Why:
The answer misses the executor-clause trigger once a personal collateral undertaking is alleged.

Example 4
Facts:
Note is signed "A, Administrator," but surrounding language suggests a personal undertaking.
Bad answer:
"Title alone means there can be no personal liability."
Primary label:
E10C
Why:
Capacity language is relevant but not automatically dispositive.

Example 5
Facts:
Answer spends its main analysis on land part performance or UCC merchant confirmation.
Primary label:
E00A or E40A
Why:
The answer is either misrouted or importing the wrong pack's doctrine.

DRAFTING REMINDERS FOR CLEAN PACK-30 ANSWERS
A clean answer in this pack usually does all of the following:
- states the executor/administrator personal-promise doctrine as the controlling doctrine when appropriate
- separates personal capacity from representative capacity
- separates decedent or estate debt from the representative's own administration obligation
- explains why the writing requirement is or is not triggered
- analyzes any memorandum without inventing facts
- keeps fallback theories separate and source-supported
- states the strongest counterargument
- omits specific uncertainty inside "Strongest counterargument:" when capacity, assets, or the exact undertaking are not fully pinned down
