LAND CONTRACTS DOCTRINE PACK
For Gold-Packet Drafting, Benchmark Answers, and Failure-Mode Drafting

PURPOSE
This file is the Pack 20 substantive doctrine guide for questions whose likely controlling issue is whether an agreement to transfer, create, lease, option, mortgage, or pay for an interest in land is enforceable despite the Statute of Frauds or a writing defect. It is not itself legal authority. It is a reasoning aid for source extraction, benchmark-answer drafting, and failure-mode identification.

SCOPE
This pack is intentionally narrower than “real estate law” generally. It is designed for:
- land-sale or conveyance agreements
- options or promises to buy or sell land
- leases or agreements to lease where duration may trigger the land-writing rule
- easements or other interests in land where the writing requirement is central
- questions about whether a memorandum or related writings sufficiently satisfy the land Statute of Frauds
- questions about part performance, estoppel, restitution, or full-performance theories offered to get around a missing or defective writing

Do not force-fit:
- title, recording, bona fide purchaser, deed-delivery, or closing-priority disputes
- pure construction or service agreements with no transfer of a land interest
- mixed goods, fixture, crop, or severance questions that are really UCC or goods-characterization problems
- executor, suretyship, marriage-consideration, or goods-confirmation problems just because land appears in the facts
- brokerage or commission statutes, cohabitation property disputes, trust disputes, or corporate-authority problems unless the source clearly makes the land-writing issue primary
- short-term lease disputes if the governing source clearly excepts that lease term from the land-writing rule

ROUTING TRIGGER
Use Pack 20 when the best description of the controlling issue is:
- real property transfer or land interest
- oral or defective writing for sale, lease, option, easement, mortgage, or other land interest
- sufficiency of memorandum or description of land
- part performance or equitable workaround to enforce an oral land deal

If mixed or unclear, do source extraction first and name the best-fit pack without forcing a final benchmark answer.

USE RULE
Treat the uploaded case, statute, Restatement section, or doctrine source as the substantive authority. Use this pack as a default framing aid only. If the source conflicts with this pack, the source controls.

DEFAULT FRAMING
- Default to U.S. common law / Restatement-style framing unless the source requires a specific jurisdiction.
- Focus on enforceability, not just proof.
- Keep transaction characterization, writing compliance, and equitable workarounds analytically separate.
- Distinguish a missing writing from a defective or partial writing.
- Distinguish legal remedies from equitable remedies when a substitute doctrine is raised.
- Do not invent legal descriptions, deeds, signed memoranda, agency authority, possession, improvements, payment, or closing facts.
- Do not assume one land-related fact makes the entire problem a land-contracts case; characterize the transaction first.

SCOPE AND TRIGGER
Core trigger:
- A promise to transfer, buy, create, assign, lease, option, mortgage, or otherwise convey an interest in land is usually within the land provision of the Statute of Frauds.
- Pack 20 usually applies when the enforceability question turns on whether the alleged agreement concerns an interest in land and whether the writing requirement is met or excused.

What usually fits:
- sale or purchase of land or a building
- option to purchase land
- contract to convey land after some stated condition is met
- lease or agreement to lease where the relevant statute requires a writing
- easement or other continuing land interest if the writing issue is central
- oral land sale followed by possession, payment, or improvements
- later conveyance or full-performance issues affecting the price claim
- emails, letters, checks, texts, drafts, or related writings offered as the memorandum

What usually does NOT fit:
- pure deed-interpretation or recording disputes after a concededly valid transfer
- construction or renovation agreements that do not actually transfer a land interest
- sale of severed goods, fixtures, crops, or manufactured components when the real issue is UCC or goods characterization
- oral promises merely secured by land but not transferring an interest in land
- purely procedural broker-commission or licensing statutes unless the source makes them the primary issue
- loose family-property fairness disputes with no coherent contract-to-transfer-land theory

MUST-SEPARATE SUBISSUES
1. Transaction characterization
Ask first what the alleged agreement actually is:
- sale, option, lease, easement, mortgage, or something else
- transfer of an interest in land versus service contract with land in the background
- present transfer, future promise to convey, or promise to pay after conveyance

2. Trigger question
Separate:
- whether the agreement is of a type covered by the land writing rule
from
- whether the existing documents satisfy that rule

3. Writing sufficiency
Separate:
- existence of some writing
from
- whether the writing identifies the parties, the land, the nature of the transaction, and the essential terms with sufficient definiteness under the relevant source or jurisdiction

4. Property description
Do not bury this inside generic writing analysis.
Ask:
- Is the property description definite enough on the face of the writing or by reference to another identified instrument?
- Is the answer trying to use oral testimony to supply, rather than merely explain, a missing description?
- Is the jurisdiction unusually strict about legal description?

5. Signature, party-to-be-charged, and agency
Separate:
- whether the writing is signed by the party against whom enforcement is sought
from
- whether an agent signed, and whether written authority is required under the relevant jurisdiction or source

6. Remedy posture
Separate:
- claim for specific performance
- claim for damages or price
- fallback restitution or unjust enrichment
The same facts do not necessarily support every remedy equally.

7. Substitute or exception theory
Keep each workaround tied to the barrier it is supposed to cure:
- part performance
- full performance or conveyance
- equitable estoppel
- promissory estoppel
- restitution or unjust enrichment
Do not let one fallback theory dissolve all writing problems without fit analysis.

WRITING SATISFACTION REQUIREMENTS
Core rule:
- The land-contract writing requirement is usually satisfied only if there is a signed writing or memorandum sufficient to indicate that the parties made the relevant land agreement.
- Under many common-law formulations, the writing must identify the parties, the land interest or property, and the essential bargain with enough definiteness that the court is not forced to invent the contract from oral proof alone.

Practical writing questions:
- Who signed?
- What exact land or interest is being transferred?
- Is the document a final agreement, a memorandum, or a chain of related writings?
- Does the writing state the price or a workable method of fixing it, if the source or jurisdiction treats price as essential?
- Are time, closing, or payment terms stated with enough certainty, or is the source tolerant of gap filling?
- Is the alleged memorandum actually multiple documents that can be read together?
- Does the answer rely on a document that postdates the deal, and if so, does it actually memorialize the contract rather than create a new one?

Guardrails:
- Do not assume any mention of an address or parcel is enough; description sufficiency is often a live issue.
- Do not assume any signed note automatically satisfies the land writing rule.
- Do not use parol evidence to create a contract the writing does not itself identify.
- Do not assume strict universality on essential terms; flag jurisdiction or source sensitivity instead.

SUBSTITUTES / EXCEPTIONS / FALLBACK THEORIES
Use only after the main trigger and writing questions.

1. Part performance
Core idea:
- Many jurisdictions recognize part performance as an equitable basis to enforce an oral land contract when the claimant’s conduct is strongly referable to the alleged agreement.

Recurring indicators:
- possession
- payment of all or part of the price
- substantial improvements

But formulations vary. Some jurisdictions demand a combination of these; others focus on whether the conduct is unequivocally referable to the alleged land contract.

Guardrails:
- Payment alone is often not enough.
- Possession alone is often not enough if explainable on another ground.
- Improvements must be linked to the alleged bargain, not merely normal use of the property.
- Part performance is often most natural in specific-performance posture, not as a universal damages cure.

2. Full performance / completed transfer
Core idea:
- Once the land interest has actually been conveyed, some authorities treat the buyer’s or transferee’s promise to pay as no longer within the land provision in the same way.

Use carefully:
- Identify who fully performed and what remains to be enforced.
- Distinguish completed conveyance from mere preparation to convey.
- Do not confuse full performance with a still-executory oral promise.

3. Equitable estoppel / promissory estoppel
Core idea:
- Estoppel may sometimes prevent a party from using the Statute of Frauds opportunistically, but it is not a universal override.

What to analyze:
- inducement
- reasonable reliance
- concrete change of position
- whether the jurisdiction or source allows estoppel to overcome this type of land writing defect
- whether the remedy is limited to avoiding injustice rather than full expectation enforcement

Guardrails:
- Do not say fairness defeats the statute.
- Do not treat ordinary negotiation costs or subjective expectations as enough by themselves.
- Do not let estoppel swallow a description or signature defect without source support.

4. Restitution / unjust enrichment
Core idea:
- Even if the land contract is unenforceable, a party who conferred value may have a non-contract remedy.

Use this as a fallback remedy question, not as proof that the contract itself is enforceable.

5. Other workarounds
Use only if the source clearly supports them:
- admission
- constructive trust
- reformation tied to an existing writing
- waiver or later ratification

Do not insert these as generic labels without fit analysis.

TYPICAL BOUNDED-UNCERTAINTY POINTS
The main recurring uncertainty points in land-writing cases are:
- whether the alleged agreement actually transfers an interest in land rather than only involving land in the background
- whether the jurisdiction requires especially strict property-description language
- whether price or payment terms are treated as essential in the relevant source or jurisdiction
- whether related documents can be read together as one memorandum
- whether an agent’s authority must itself be in writing
- whether the lease term falls inside a short-term statutory carveout
- whether the claimant’s acts are sufficiently referable to the alleged contract for part performance
- whether estoppel is allowed to overcome the relevant land-writing defect and, if so, for what remedy
- whether completed conveyance or full performance changes the statute analysis for the remaining payment obligation

PACK-SPECIFIC MODEL FAILURE MODES
Watch especially for these recurring mistakes:
- Misclassifies the transaction: treats a service or construction deal or a UCC or fixtures problem as a land-transfer case, or vice versa.
- Any-real-estate-mention trigger: assumes that any reference to land automatically means the land Statute of Frauds controls.
- Writing-as-proof-only error: treats the missing writing as just an evidentiary weakness instead of an enforceability barrier.
- Description-blindness: says the writing is sufficient without testing whether the land is described with enough definiteness.
- Essential-terms gloss: assumes a vague email or note satisfies the statute without analyzing price, payment, closing, or nature of the interest when the source requires them.
- Invented memorandum: adds a signed writing, deed, legal description, parcel number, or closing document not in the facts or source.
- Agency shortcut: assumes an agent could sign without considering whether authority must also be in writing.
- Payment-alone part performance: treats part payment by itself as automatically enough.
- Possession-alone part performance: treats possession by itself as automatically enough even when independently explainable.
- Universal part-performance cure: assumes that part performance always makes the oral land contract fully enforceable for every remedy.
- Estoppel-as-universal-cure: uses equitable or promissory estoppel without jurisdiction-sensitive limits.
- Remedy confusion: jumps from specific-performance-friendly facts to full damages at law without analysis.
- Full-performance overread: says any substantial step toward closing eliminates the Statute of Frauds.
- Parol-evidence overuse: uses oral testimony to supply omitted property or core terms instead of only clarifying an existing writing.
- No specific uncertainty under "Strongest counterargument:" says only “it depends” instead of naming the exact source of variability.

ANSWER-STYLE DEFAULTS
When drafting from this pack:
- Characterize the transaction first.
- Name the likely land-writing trigger early.
- Separate the trigger question from the sufficiency-of-writing question.
- Address description and essential-terms issues explicitly when they matter.
- Keep equitable substitutes after the main writing analysis.
- Distinguish enforceability of the contract from non-contract remedies.
- Keep remedy posture visible.
- Use full doctrine names and neutral black-letter-law style.
- Preserve factual fidelity.
- If the source is too jurisdiction-specific or split-sensitive to generalize, say so and stop at source extraction plus JD review flags.
