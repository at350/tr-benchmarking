PACK 40 — SALE-OF-GOODS (UCC § 2-201) FAILURE-MODE BANK
Reusable label bank for benchmark answers, cluster summaries, and rubric-adjacent JD review

PURPOSE
This file is the Pack 40 failure bank for sale-of-goods writing questions under UCC section 2-201. It is intended to fit the reorganized doctrine-pack structure while keeping failure labeling aligned with the shared workflow. Use it to support answer comparison, clustering, and failure tagging across Pack 40 questions centered on goods characterization, signed writing plus quantity, the merchant-confirmation rule, and the specially manufactured goods / admission / payment-receipt-and-acceptance exceptions.

SCOPE
This bank covers Pack 40 — Sale of goods (UCC § 2-201), especially questions involving:
- whether the transaction is actually a sale of goods rather than services or another doctrine family
- whether the usual UCC section 2-201 threshold is triggered
- whether an existing writing or memorandum sufficiently indicates a contract and states quantity
- whether the merchant-confirmation rule can satisfy the statute
- whether specially manufactured goods, admission, or payment / receipt and acceptance remove the writing bar
- whether a later modification or quantity change must itself satisfy the statute

Typical Pack 40 prompts include:
- oral or thinly documented sales of goods
- mixed goods-services transactions with a writing issue
- merchant-to-merchant confirmation disputes
- custom-goods orders
- oral quantity increases after a signed base agreement
- partial payment, shipment, delivery, receipt, or acceptance disputes in goods contracts

Routing note:
- Some Pack 40 prompts will turn mainly on goods characterization or mixed-transaction scope.
- Others will turn mainly on writing sufficiency and quantity.
- Others will turn mainly on the merchant-confirmation rule or a specific UCC exception.
- Route by the actual controlling issue, not by the mere fact that merchandise appears somewhere in the facts.

If the problem is mainly about a separate guaranty, land transfer, executor promise, or another non-UCC-writing issue, do not force-fit the answer into these labels. Instead use:
- OUT-1 Outside current UCC 2-201 bank scope

CONTINUITY / COMPATIBILITY NOTE
- This file follows the same stage order and label style used in Pack 10 and Pack 20.
- It keeps shared label families (SG / SC / WS / XD / OUT) and adds Pack 40-specific families (GC / MF / UE / OD) so exports, clustering workflows, and rubric-adjacent labeling remain aligned across packs.

USE RULES
- Assign metadata tags first.
- Then assign one primary doctrinal failure label.
- Add up to two secondary modifiers if needed.
- Prefer the earliest doctrinal gate that goes materially wrong:
  structural gatekeeping -> goods characterization -> Statute of Frauds core / gate selection -> writing sufficiency / quantity -> merchant-confirmation or specific UCC exception -> other defenses / competing doctrines -> cross-cutting discipline.
- Do not punish the same mistake twice under different labels.
- Use this bank for failure labeling, not as substantive authority.
- When a specific source case or doctrine source conflicts with this bank, the source controls.

PRIMARY FAILURE-MODE LABELS

A. STRUCTURAL GATEKEEPING
Use when the answer’s overall path is wrong before the doctrine-specific analysis even begins.

SG-1 Controlling-doctrine omission
Apply when the answer never identifies UCC section 2-201 or another controlling Pack 40 writing issue even though the enforceability question is centered on a goods contract.
Typical cues:
- jumps straight to delivery, warranty, remedies, or fairness
- mentions “no writing” without naming the UCC sale-of-goods category
- lists Article 2 doctrines without identifying which one controls

SG-2 Fallback-first / misordered analysis
Apply when the answer leads with estoppel, fairness, good faith, or some secondary doctrine before goods characterization and the main UCC writing analysis.
Typical cues:
- equitable discussion appears before the section 2-201 trigger question
- the answer starts with merchant-confirmation or exception language before establishing that Pack 40 is the right frame

SG-3 Laundry-list without prioritization
Apply when the answer names multiple UCC or contract doctrines but never identifies the controlling path.
Typical cues:
- section 2-201, battle of the forms, warranty, and remedies all appear with no ranking
- no path from goods characterization to writing sufficiency to exceptions

SG-4 Barrier collapse / exception bleed-over
Apply when the answer treats one fact or one workaround as solving every writing problem.
Typical cues:
- “they are merchants, so the oral contract is enforceable” with no analysis of quantity or confirmation elements
- “there was partial performance, so all section 2-201 problems disappear”

SG-5 No bottom line / strongest-counterargument-specificity failure
Apply when the answer gives no clear conclusion, gives conflicting conclusions, or hides behind vague uncertainty instead of resolving the section 2-201 issue.
Typical cues:
- generic “it depends under the UCC” language with no identified source of uncertainty
- application and conclusion point in opposite directions

B. TRANSACTION / GOODS CHARACTERIZATION
Use when the answer mishandles whether the transaction is actually a Pack 40 sale-of-goods problem.

GC-1 Goods-characterization miss
Apply when the answer fails to recognize that the transaction is a sale of goods governed by Article 2.
Typical cues:
- treats an ordinary contract to buy movable goods as purely common-law services analysis
- never asks whether Article 2 is the governing frame

GC-2 Mixed-transaction / predominant-purpose miss
Apply when the answer assumes Article 2 applies or does not apply merely because both goods and services appear, without analyzing the transaction’s main thrust.
Typical cues:
- installation, manufacturing, repair, or creative-services contract treated as automatically a goods sale
- goods component ignored even though the transfer of goods is the transaction’s main point

GC-3 Non-goods subject-matter confusion
Apply when the answer treats non-goods subject matter as though it were clearly within Article 2, or ignores subject-matter facts that bear on whether the item is a movable good.
Typical cues:
- land or a nonmovable item treated as a good with no analysis
- no attention to whether the subject matter is actually the kind of movable property Article 2 covers

GC-4 Collateral-promise / wrong-transaction misclassification
Apply when the answer treats a separate financing, guaranty, or side promise as though the litigated issue were the goods sale itself.
Typical cues:
- goods are in the background, but the real dispute is about a separate promise to pay or support performance
- answer analyzes section 2-201 when another pack’s collateral-promise issue is doing the real work

C. STATUTE OF FRAUDS CORE / GATE SELECTION
Use when the answer mishandles the general section 2-201 framework or selects the wrong gate.

SC-1 SoF-as-proof-only error
Apply when the answer treats the missing writing as only an evidentiary or credibility problem rather than an enforceability bar.
Typical cues:
- “hard to prove” rather than “unenforceable absent a sufficient writing or exception”
- oral sale enforced simply because the court could believe the witnesses

SC-2 Wrong controlling writing regime
Apply when the answer chooses the wrong governing writing framework or ignores the UCC section 2-201 frame.
Typical cues:
- treats the case primarily as common-law one-year, land, or generic contract law when the real issue is UCC section 2-201
- never identifies the sale-of-goods writing rule as the main controller

SC-3 Trigger / threshold test misstated
Apply when the answer states the wrong trigger for section 2-201.
Typical cues:
- says every sale of goods requires a writing regardless of the usual threshold
- ignores the usual threshold entirely
- states the threshold incorrectly or applies it to the wrong transaction

SC-4 Separate barriers collapsed
Apply when the answer runs trigger, writing sufficiency, quantity, merchant confirmation, and exceptions together without separating them.
Typical cues:
- one paragraph says “there was enough paperwork and performance” with no analysis of what each fact proves
- no explanation of whether the problem is trigger, sufficiency, confirmation, or exception

SC-5 Unsupported writing-substitute / memorandum claim
Apply when the answer invents or overreads a writing, memorandum, signature, or substitute-compliance theory not supported by the source or facts.
Typical cues:
- assumes an invoice, email, purchase order, or check automatically satisfies section 2-201 without fit analysis
- invents a signed order, written objection, or linked memoranda not in the record

D. WRITING SUFFICIENCY / QUANTITY
Use when the answer reaches the right general gate but mishandles what the writing must contain or who must sign.

WS-1 Wrong signer / party-to-be-charged confusion
Apply when the answer ignores the need for a writing signed by the party against whom enforcement is sought or attributes the signature requirement to the wrong side.
Typical cues:
- unsigned writing treated as enough because the other side performed
- signature by the enforcing party treated as automatically sufficient against the non-signer

WS-2 Quantity-term omission blind spot
Apply when the answer ignores that section 2-201 ordinarily requires a quantity term and that enforceability is generally limited to the quantity stated.
Typical cues:
- writing with no quantity treated as fully sufficient
- oral testimony used to supply the missing quantity as though quantity were just a gap-filler

WS-3 Price-required-in-writing error
Apply when the answer treats missing price or another non-quantity term as automatically fatal under section 2-201, even though Article 2 may tolerate some omitted terms while still insisting on quantity.
Typical cues:
- “no price, so no enforceable contract under section 2-201” with no quantity analysis
- writing rejected solely because payment details or delivery date are omitted

WS-4 Hypertechnical formal-document error
Apply when the answer assumes the statute can be satisfied only by one formal integrated contract and ignores that a memorandum can be informal.
Typical cues:
- rejects invoices, purchase orders, confirmation letters, emails, or checks solely because they are not a formal signed contract
- assumes no formal contract means no possible compliance

WS-5 Writing-sufficiency overclaim
Apply when the answer treats any mention of goods and dollars as automatically sufficient and never analyzes whether the writing actually indicates a contract and states quantity.
Typical cues:
- “the invoice mentions the goods and price, so the statute is satisfied” with no further analysis
- no distinction between some evidence of negotiations and a sufficient memorandum

WS-6 Output / requirements quantity confusion
Apply when the answer says an output or requirements term is automatically too indefinite, or automatically sufficient, without analyzing whether the writing actually states that kind of commitment and how quantity is bounded.
Typical cues:
- “requirements contract means section 2-201 is no problem” with no further analysis
- “no fixed number means no quantity term” where the writing may state an output or requirements commitment

E. MERCHANT-CONFIRMATION LABELS
Use only if the merchant-confirmation rule is actually in play.

MF-1 Merchant-status blind spot
Apply when the answer fails to ask whether both parties are merchants for the merchant-confirmation rule or assumes that any commercial party automatically qualifies.
Typical cues:
- one merchant is treated as enough
- no analysis of whether the recipient is also a merchant

MF-2 Confirmatory-memorandum outside merchant-to-merchant context
Apply when the answer uses the merchant-confirmation rule against a nonmerchant or consumer recipient.
Typical cues:
- consumer buyer’s silence treated as satisfying section 2-201
- nonmerchant recipient treated as bound by the 10-day objection rule

MF-3 Confirmation-elements miss
Apply when the answer invokes merchant confirmation without testing the rule’s required elements.
Typical cues:
- no discussion of whether the writing confirms an already-made contract
- no discussion of whether the writing would satisfy the statute as against the sender
- no discussion of receipt or reason to know the writing’s contents

MF-4 Ten-day written-objection rule omitted or misstated
Apply when the answer ignores or misstates the requirement that the recipient object in writing within the required period.
Typical cues:
- oral objection treated as enough with no support
- silence treated as irrelevant even though the rule is otherwise satisfied
- wrong timing or no timing analysis at all

MF-5 Confirmation-creates-contract confusion
Apply when the answer treats a confirmatory memorandum as creating the contract or accepting new terms, rather than as a statute-of-frauds device for an already-made contract.
Typical cues:
- silence after confirmation treated as acceptance of a new offer
- merchant-confirmation analysis merged into battle-of-the-forms analysis with no distinction

F. UCC EXCEPTIONS / SUBSTITUTES
Use after the main writing analysis.

UE-1 Special-manufacture overread
Apply when the answer treats any customized or branded goods as automatically taking the contract outside section 2-201.
Typical cues:
- custom color, logo, or specifications treated as enough by themselves
- no discussion of whether the goods are suitable for sale to others in the ordinary course of business

UE-2 Special-manufacture prerequisites missed
Apply when the answer invokes the specially manufactured goods exception without testing its core prerequisites.
Typical cues:
- no discussion of whether the seller substantially began manufacture or made a commitment to procure
- no discussion of whether the seller acted before receiving notice of repudiation
- no discussion of whether the goods are truly unsuitable for ordinary resale

UE-3 Admission exception overread
Apply when the answer treats an informal statement or broad concession as enough to satisfy the admission exception, or ignores the exception’s quantity limit.
Typical cues:
- out-of-court negotiation statement treated as enough
- “they admitted a contract existed” used with no analysis of where or how the admission occurred
- no discussion of how much quantity was admitted

UE-4 Payment / receipt-and-acceptance overread
Apply when the answer treats tender, shipment, invoicing, or offered payment as enough without actual payment accepted or goods received and accepted.
Typical cues:
- “the seller shipped, so the statute is satisfied” with no receipt-and-acceptance analysis
- offered payment or a promised deposit treated the same as payment made and accepted
- partial performance treated as a universal cure

UE-5 Exception-quantity-limitation miss
Apply when the answer uses admission or payment / receipt and acceptance to enforce more goods than the exception actually reaches.
Typical cues:
- partial payment treated as validating the whole oral contract with no quantity limit
- admission of some quantity treated as enough for all goods claimed

G. OTHER DEFENSES / COMPETING DOCTRINES
Use for secondary doctrines that commonly displace or distort the actual section 2-201 question.

OD-1 Gap-filler misuse
Apply when the answer uses Article 2 gap-fillers to supply a missing quantity or otherwise bypass section 2-201’s actual writing requirements.
Typical cues:
- “courts can fill the missing quantity the same way they fill price”
- general course-of-dealing or commercial-reasonableness language used as a substitute for a required quantity term

OD-2 Battle-of-the-forms drift
Apply when the answer turns a section 2-201 writing-enforceability problem into a section 2-207 acceptance / additional-terms problem without support.
Typical cues:
- focus on mirror-image or additional-terms analysis when the real issue is whether there is a sufficient writing for enforcement
- merchant-confirmation discussion blurred into battle-of-the-forms doctrine

OD-3 Modification / quantity-change confusion
Apply when the answer mishandles whether a later modification that brings the deal into the statute, or changes quantity, must itself satisfy the writing requirement.
Typical cues:
- signed initial contract treated as automatically validating all later oral quantity changes
- any oral modification treated as automatically invalid without analyzing whether the modification actually changes the quantity or otherwise implicates section 2-201

OD-4 Estoppel / fairness free-float
Apply when the answer uses promissory estoppel, good faith, course of dealing, or fairness as a free-standing override to section 2-201 without source-supported fit.
Typical cues:
- “the UCC values commercial fairness, so the oral contract should be enforced”
- no attention to whether the selected source or jurisdiction actually allows the asserted workaround to do that work

OD-5 Warranty / tender / remedy drift
Apply when the answer stops doing threshold writing analysis and pivots into perfect tender, warranties, cover, damages, or other downstream Article 2 remedies.
Typical cues:
- answer assumes an enforceable contract and grades performance issues without resolving section 2-201 first
- warranty or remedy discussion displaces the writing-enforceability question

H. CROSS-CUTTING DISCIPLINE
Use for mistakes that cut across the doctrinal analysis.

XD-1 Invented facts or documents
Apply when the answer adds writings, signatures, purchase orders, confirmations, written objections, merchant status facts, quantity terms, admissions, payment facts, or acceptance facts not in the source or prompt.

XD-2 Party-role confusion
Apply when the answer mixes up buyer, seller, sender, recipient, merchant, nonmerchant, manufacturer, reseller, or customer roles in a way that affects the analysis.

XD-3 Quantity / threshold / timing error
Apply when the answer misstated the amount in dispute, the usual threshold, the quantity admitted or paid for, the 10-day objection timing, or the sequence of manufacture, repudiation, shipment, receipt, or acceptance.

XD-4 Jurisdiction drift
Apply when the answer imports jurisdiction-specific Article 2 rules, amendments, or estoppel doctrines without stating that assumption or treats a local rule as universal.

XD-5 Empty hedging / unsupported universals
Apply when the answer uses vague “depends on the state” language without identifying the real split, or uses broad universal claims about merchants, informal writings, or UCC flexibility without support.

XD-6 Off-task commercial-advice or remedy drift
Apply when the answer stops doing enforceability analysis and turns into contract-drafting advice, ordering advice, litigation strategy, damages strategy, or generalized commercial counseling.

XD-7 Internal contradiction
Apply when the answer’s issue / rule / application path points one way but the conclusion points the other, or when the answer simultaneously says there is no sufficient writing and that the writing clearly satisfies section 2-201 without reconciliation.

OUTSIDE-SCOPE LABEL
OUT-1 Outside current UCC 2-201 bank scope
Apply when the problem primarily turns on another doctrine pack or on an Article 2 issue other than the sale-of-goods writing requirement.
Typical cues:
- services dominate a mixed transaction and there is no real Pack 40 writing issue
- separate guaranty or suretyship promise controls
- land-transfer or executor-promise issue controls
- battle of the forms, warranties, perfect tender, risk of loss, parol evidence, or remedies is the real controller rather than section 2-201

RECOMMENDED LABEL SYNTAX
[Metadata tags] + [Primary failure label] + [Optional secondary modifiers]

Examples:
- Not enforceable / Correct / Right result-wrong reason + WS-2 Quantity-term omission blind spot
- Enforceable / Incorrect / Wrong result-plausible reasoning + MF-2 Confirmatory-memorandum outside merchant-to-merchant context
- Mixed / Indeterminate / Wrong result-poor reasoning + SG-1 Controlling-doctrine omission + XD-5 Empty hedging

QUICK JD REVIEW RULE
When in doubt, ask:
1. What is the earliest gate that went materially wrong: goods characterization, section 2-201 trigger, writing sufficiency / quantity, merchant confirmation, or exception fit?
2. Is the answer using UCC gap-fillers, merchant status, or fairness to do work that section 2-201 does not automatically allow?
3. Would assigning a second label double-count the same failure?
4. Is the question actually within Pack 40, or is another pack or another Article 2 doctrine doing the real work?
