Lead source illustration: Anglemire v. Policemen's Benev. Ass'n of Chicago (Illinois).

WORKED BENCHMARK ANSWER

Jurisdiction assumption:
[Supported by source] Illinois law, narrowly grounded in Anglemire's treatment of oral promises made upon consideration of marriage in the benefit-certificate setting.

Bottom-line outcome:
[Supported by source] The later-named beneficiaries have the better claim. The spouse's claim depends on an oral premarital promise made in consideration of marriage, and under the Illinois rule reflected in Anglemire that promise is unenforceable absent a writing.

Controlling doctrine:
[Supported by source] The controlling doctrine is the Statute of Frauds category for an agreement made upon consideration of marriage.
[Supported by source] A verbal executory promise made upon consideration of marriage must be in writing to be enforceable.
[Supported by source] Later marriage does not, by itself, count as part performance that removes the agreement from the statute.

Formation:
[Inference from source] There is at least a plausible bargain framing: the promisor allegedly offered to name the promisee as beneficiary if she married him, and she alleges that she married in reliance on that promise.
[Inference from source] But formation is not the dispositive issue in this source. Even assuming arguendo that a bargain existed, enforceability still fails at the Statute of Frauds stage.

Statute of Frauds Gates:
[Supported by source] The marriage-consideration gate is dispositive. The alleged promise was made before marriage and was expressly tied to the promisee's marrying the promisor, so marriage was the consideration for the promise.
[Supported by source] Under Anglemire, that kind of oral promise is void unless reduced to writing.
[Supported by source] The later marriage does not cure the defect because marriage in this setting is the exact performance contemplated by the statute and does not qualify as part performance taking the agreement outside the statute.
[Supported by source] The promisor's later act of obtaining a certificate naming the spouse beneficiary does not cure the original writing defect when the underlying oral promise is unenforceable.
[Inference from source] Secondary Statute of Frauds gates such as one-year performance or suretyship are not the real issue on these facts and should not be expanded into major sections.

Exceptions/Promissory Estoppel:
[Supported by source] The strongest source-grounded fallback is an estoppel / executed-contract theory: after the marriage, the promisor did obtain a certificate naming the spouse as beneficiary, and the spouse retained possession of it.
[Supported by source] Anglemire rejects that move. Once the underlying oral marriage-consideration promise is treated as void, the later beneficiary designation does not become irrevocable simply because the promisor once carried it out.
[Background generalization] A broader modern promissory-estoppel analysis should not be substituted for the source unless the user asks for cross-jurisdictional generalization. If discussed at all, it should be flagged as a generalization rather than source language.

Defenses/Mistake:
[Supported by source] There is no meaningful mistake issue in this source.
[Supported by source] The key non-contract defense point is procedural only in a limited sense: the association's bylaw mechanism for replacing a certificate when the old one could not be surrendered matters only after the spouse's oral-promise theory fails.
[Inference from source] This section should stay brief because the source is driven by the Statute of Frauds analysis, not by independent mistake or excuse doctrine.

Strongest counterargument:
[Supported by source] The strongest counterargument is that the arrangement became executed after marriage because the promisor actually obtained a certificate naming the spouse beneficiary, and that he therefore should be estopped from later changing the designation.
[Supported by source] That is a real doctrinal counterargument because it tracks the trial court's reasoning. But the appellate court rejected it on the ground that the original oral promise was within the Statute of Frauds and the later act did not eliminate that defect.

[Supported by source] If the task is limited to Illinois law as reflected in Anglemire, the outcome is strong.
[Inference from source] The main uncertainty is portability: another jurisdiction might treat estoppel more broadly, or might frame the significance of the later-issued certificate differently.
[Background generalization] That portability question should be flagged as jurisdiction-sensitive rather than used to weaken the source-grounded Illinois conclusion.
